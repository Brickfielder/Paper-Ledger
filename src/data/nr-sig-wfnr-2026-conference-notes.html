08:55 Caroline van Heugten Cognitive enhancement in rehabilitation: hype or hope? Session 1: NR-SIG Chair KeynoteKeynote
Presenter: Caroline van Heugten Programme time: Sunday 19 July 2026, 08:55 Note captured 09:34

Overview

The speaker reviewed cognitive enhancement approaches in neurorehabilitation, particularly after acute brain injuries such as stroke, traumatic brain injury, and cardiac arrest. The talk placed these approaches within modern cognitive rehabilitation, neuroplasticity, the ICF framework, and the distinction between efficacy and real-world effectiveness.

Main Arguments

  • Cognitive rehabilitation includes interventions addressing cognitive, emotional, behavioral, social, family, activity, participation, and environmental consequences of brain injury.
  • Neuroplasticity may be stimulated, but clinically meaningful benefit requires changes in behavior, daily functioning, and participation—not merely increased brain activity or improved performance on trained tasks.
  • Learning and transfer are task-specific. Computerized cognitive training often improves the trained task, with limited evidence of generalization to everyday functioning.
  • Cognitive enhancement should generally be considered an adjunct to conventional rehabilitation rather than a replacement for it.
  • Clinicians should consider accessibility, safety, side effects, privacy, patient preferences, and their own treatment preferences when discussing commercially available enhancement products.

Key Findings

  • Evidence for cognitive enhancement in brain-injury rehabilitation remains limited and mixed.
  • Methylphenidate was identified as the pharmacological intervention with the clearest support in the cited traumatic-brain-injury guidance and reviews.
  • Virtual and augmented reality appear promising, particularly for engaging, intensive, variable practice, but the extent of transfer to daily life remains uncertain.
  • Non-invasive brain stimulation has growing but inconclusive evidence, with potential value as an add-on to cognitive rehabilitation. Some cited evidence suggested effects on working memory and alertness, mainly in domain-specific ways.
  • Evidence for neurofeedback in brain-injury rehabilitation was described as limited and insufficient, based on only four studies in the cited review.
  • Physical fitness may improve physical disability outcomes, but the cited Cochrane review did not establish effects on cognition because cognitive outcomes were not adequately measured.
  • Evidence for enriched environments, nutrition, nootropic or “smart” drugs, and psychedelics in brain-injury rehabilitation was described as insufficient or preliminary.
  • Commercial availability is outpacing the evidence base, meaning patients may use these interventions even when clinical support is weak.

Methods Or Evidence

  • The speaker conducted a literature review for the presentation and discussed published reviews, meta-analyses, Cochrane reviews, clinical guidelines, and an international handbook on neuropsychological rehabilitation.
  • Evidence was discussed across pharmacotherapy, computerized cognitive training, virtual and augmented reality, non-invasive brain stimulation, neurofeedback, physical fitness, enriched environments, nutrition, nootropics, and psychedelics.
  • The talk emphasized the distinction between efficacy—whether an intervention can produce an effect under study conditions—and effectiveness—whether it improves daily functioning and participation.
  • Examples from ordinary learning were used to illustrate near transfer, far transfer, positive transfer, negative transfer, and the need for explicit metacognitive support for generalization.
  • The speaker noted that many studies are small, outcomes are inconsistent, and long-term effects and adverse effects are frequently under-studied.

Practical Or Clinical Implications

  • Continue emphasizing evidence-based neuropsychological rehabilitation targeting activities, participation, personal factors, and environmental factors.
  • Treat cognitive enhancement technologies and stimulation methods cautiously as potential adjuncts, not established stand-alone treatments.
  • Ask patients what outcomes and interventions they value, and discuss uncertainty without creating false hope.
  • Be prepared to advise patients about interventions they can obtain online, including their uncertain efficacy, possible adverse effects, privacy concerns, and accessibility.
  • Assess whether an intervention improves meaningful behavior and everyday functioning rather than only performance on a trained task or laboratory measure.
  • Consider clinician and researcher preferences as potential sources of bias in treatment selection.

Limitations And Uncertainties

  • The speaker did not provide complete bibliographic details, sample sizes, effect estimates, or intervention protocols for the cited studies.
  • Evidence was repeatedly characterized as mixed, limited, domain-specific, preliminary, or insufficient.
  • Generalization from laboratory or training tasks to daily functioning and participation remains uncertain, especially for brain training and immersive technologies.
  • Long-term benefits and side effects have not been adequately established for many approaches.
  • Some areas, including smart drugs, nutrition, enriched environments, and psychedelics, have substantial public or research interest but little direct evidence in brain-injury rehabilitation.
  • The talk included informal examples and acknowledged occasional uncertainty about specific presentation details and translations.

  • No audience questions were included in the transcript.

  • The cited international handbook on neuropsychological rehabilitation, first published in 2017, and its forthcoming open-access successor mentioned by the speaker.
  • Australian INCOG guidelines for cognitive rehabilitation after traumatic brain injury.
  • Reviews and meta-analyses of virtual and augmented reality in neurorehabilitation.
  • Reviews of non-invasive brain stimulation as an adjunct to cognitive rehabilitation.

Overview

The speaker presented three years of post-COVID outcome data from WOCAS, a neuropsychology-led tier 4 neurorehabilitation day patient service within the St George’s, Epsom and St Helier Hospital Group in London. The service aims to provide intensive specialist rehabilitation for patients who do not require an acute inpatient bed.

Main Arguments

  • A structured day patient model can provide rehabilitation intensity and outcomes comparable to specialised inpatient neurorehabilitation.
  • The model may help address inpatient bed and workforce pressures while supporting care closer to home.
  • The service appears scalable, although the presentation did not provide detailed evidence on implementation requirements or costs.

Key Findings

  • The evaluation included 68 patients over three years.
  • The day patient cohort had a similar level of complexity to the inpatient cohort on the PCAT scale, but required less nursing input and received more therapy hours during the day.
  • Portland scores showed statistically significant improvements across total functioning, abilities, adjustment and participation, mostly at the 0.01 level.
  • Mean total Portland T-scores fell by approximately nine points, described as roughly one standard deviation of change on the adjustment and participation indices.
  • Patients generally moved from moderate disability at admission to mild-to-moderate disability, with participation reaching the mild range by discharge.
  • Outcomes were maintained when the recent cohort was compared with the original 2018–2019 pilot cohort, despite more severe recent referrals.
  • FIM-FAM gains were described as consistent with, or exceeding, local and national inpatient benchmarks.
  • At six-month follow-up, most patients were reported to be in paid or voluntary work, education or further training, or rehabilitation.

Methods Or Evidence

  • This was described as a three-year follow-up or longitudinal evaluation rather than a formal research study.
  • The sample comprised 68 day patients treated post-COVID.
  • Outcomes were assessed using Portland scores and FIM-FAM measures.
  • Results were compared with an inpatient cohort, the 2018–2019 pilot cohort, and local and national benchmarks.
  • The service operates Monday to Friday for approximately four hours per day, integrating neuropsychology, occupational therapy, physiotherapy, speech and language therapy, and neuropsychiatry. It is colocated with an inpatient neurorehabilitation bed service.

Practical Or Clinical Implications

  • Day patient neurorehabilitation may be suitable for people needing intensive specialist input who do not require inpatient nursing or acute medical care.
  • Colocation with inpatient services can preserve access to specialist expertise.
  • The model may support greater independence, community participation and return to work, education or training.

  • The reported comparisons may be affected by differences between cohorts, including the greater severity of recent referrals.
  • The transcript does not specify exact sample sizes for the comparator cohorts, statistical tests, confidence intervals or adjustment for confounding.
  • Six-month follow-up outcomes were summarised without detailed denominators or outcome distributions.
  • Cost-effectiveness, patient perspectives and the requirements for reproducing the service elsewhere were not reported.

09:55 Ella Waldman Co-Designing an Environmental Cognitive Rehabilitation Framework for Adults with Traumatic Brain Injury Datablitz: Programme development and evaluationDatablitz DB5
Presenter: Ella Waldman Programme time: Sunday 19 July 2026, 09:55 Note captured 10:03

Overview

The talk addressed environmental approaches to cognitive rehabilitation after moderate to severe traumatic brain injury (TBI). These approaches modify the physical, social, or task-related environment to support cognition without requiring ongoing active involvement from the person with TBI. The study aimed to co-design a practical environmental cognitive rehabilitation framework integrating evidence and end-user perspectives.

Main Arguments

  • Traditional cognitive remediation often depends on a person’s ability to learn, apply, and generalise strategies, which may be compromised after moderate to severe TBI.
  • Environmental approaches may be more feasible for people with significant cognitive impairment because they reduce reliance on the individual.
  • Despite being recommended in clinical guidelines and used in practice, environmental approaches remain underdeveloped and under-researched.
  • A co-designed framework can provide clinicians with flexible guidance for selecting, implementing, and evaluating environmental strategies.

Key Findings

  • Participants identified implementation barriers involving clients, clinicians, and services.
  • Client-related barriers included reduced readiness, concerns about stigma, and limited control over the environment.
  • Clinician and service-level barriers included limited time, resources, and guidance.
  • Participants emphasised individualising strategies, involving support networks, and regularly reviewing progress.
  • They recommended collecting outcome data from people with TBI, close others, and clinicians.
  • Participants supported introducing environmental approaches early in rehabilitation and providing clinicians with practical resources and training.
  • The resulting framework has three integrative levels: foundational values, a strategy toolkit, and an implementation process.

Methods Or Evidence

  • The study used participatory co-design methods guided by the Byrne and colleagues’ co-design framework.
  • Fifteen participants from Australia took part: six people with TBI, three close others, and six clinicians from neuropsychology, speech pathology, and occupational therapy.
  • Two research partners—a person with severe TBI and a speech pathologist—helped refine study materials.
  • Two 90-minute focus groups were conducted via Zoom, with participant input and feedback gathered between sessions.
  • Content analysis identified four main categories: implementation challenges; practical considerations for strategy types; principles and processes; and evaluation and implementation needs.
  • The framework also incorporated findings from systematic and scoping reviews, which had identified a limited and fragmented evidence base with few high-quality studies.

Practical Or Clinical Implications

  • Clinicians can consider modifying environments rather than relying solely on strategy learning by the person with TBI.
  • The framework’s foundational values include environment, empowerment, networks of support, valuing strengths, individualisation, reducing complexity, and ongoing use.
  • The strategy toolkit includes prompting, communication-environment modifications, feedback, physical setting modifications, and task adaptations.
  • The proposed process involves getting to know the person, setting meaningful goals, selecting and setting up strategies, and evaluating progress.
  • Implementation may require support-network involvement, practical clinician resources, training, and early integration into rehabilitation.

Limitations And Uncertainties

  • The evidence base for environmental approaches was described as limited, fragmented, and lacking in high-quality studies.
  • The framework is preliminary and has not yet undergone effectiveness or feasibility evaluation.
  • The talk did not provide detailed outcome data from the co-design activities or describe the demographic characteristics of participants beyond their roles and locations.
  • The proposed framework’s effectiveness and feasibility remain uncertain pending the planned single-case experimental design study.

  • The talk referred to systematic and scoping reviews of environmental cognitive rehabilitation approaches, but did not provide citations.
  • The next planned study is a single-case experimental design study evaluating the framework’s preliminary effectiveness and feasibility.
10:45 Alexandra Rose A formulation-based model (FBM) for assessing mood after severe brain injury: Results of a Delphi study Session 2: Developments in assessment and evaluationPlatform presentation
Presenter: Alexandra Rose Programme time: Sunday 19 July 2026, 10:45 Note captured 11:07

Overview

The speaker described research on assessing mood and depression in people with severe brain injury, particularly when cognitive and receptive communication impairments make standard assessment difficult. The work progressed from a PhD review and clinician survey to focus groups, a formulation-based model, and a two-round Delphi consensus study.

Main Arguments

  • Mood assessment remains necessary after severe brain injury because results inform treatment and support decisions.
  • Standardised self-report and observer-rated measures may be unsuitable or difficult to interpret when cognition, language, insight, memory, or emotional awareness are impaired.
  • Symptoms of depression can overlap with neurological consequences of brain injury, creating a risk of misclassification or pathologising expected recovery experiences.
  • A formulation-based, iterative assessment process may be more appropriate than relying on a single score.

Key Findings

  • No self-report mood measures were identified as having been adequately validated for people with severe cognitive and receptive language difficulties.
  • Two observer-rated scales—the ADRX and SAD-U10—had been tentatively recommended in one small study involving 25 participants, limiting generalisability.
  • In a clinician survey, speaking with others about the patient was the predominant assessment approach; standardised measures were used more frequently by clinical psychologists than medical professionals.
  • Respondents reported that standardised measures were often not used as intended, raising questions about the validity and interpretation of resulting scores.
  • A Delphi study involving 21 experts in round one and 20 in round two reached consensus on the remaining model items after revisions. The simplified model, horizontal workflow, and accompanying manual were well received.

Methods Or Evidence

  • The speaker’s PhD included a review of existing mood measures, a survey of clinicians, and approximately five focus groups.
  • Focus groups were used to identify common clinical practices and develop a formulation-based model.
  • An online Delphi survey sought expert consensus. Participants rated model sections for usefulness and provided free-text feedback. Consensus was predefined as endorsement of the two highest Likert ratings by more than 80% of experts or a mean score above 3.5 on a five-point scale.
  • The model includes referral information; pre-assessment factors; direct, indirect, and repeated observations; assessment-period analysis; interdisciplinary discussion; formulation; intervention; and post-assessment monitoring.

Practical Or Clinical Implications

  • Clinicians should interpret standardised mood scores cautiously when a person cannot understand or complete the questions as intended.
  • Assessment should consider whether observed behaviour reflects mood symptoms, neurological sequelae, environmental factors, or a combination.
  • Information should be gathered across multiple assessments and environments to establish familiarity and a baseline.
  • Assessors should reflect on their own experience, potential bias, and the risk of projecting emotions or assumptions onto the patient.
  • Interdisciplinary discussion can help determine whether environmental changes, cognitive rehabilitation, family education, psychological support, or medication should be considered.
  • Formulations should be treated as iterative and revised when new information emerges or an intervention is not effective.

Limitations And Uncertainties

  • Existing validation evidence for standardised measures in this population is limited or absent.
  • Evidence supporting the ADRX and SAD-U10 came from a single small study with 25 participants.
  • The clinician survey and Delphi study were largely UK-based; the Delphi included mainly therapists and clinical psychologists, with two medical professionals and one neuropsychiatrist mentioned among the participants.
  • The formulation-based model, manual, and related papers were described as under review or in development, so their effectiveness and clinical outcomes have not yet been established.
  • The speaker acknowledged that the model is intentionally complex, and its feasibility and implementation requirements remain uncertain.

  • The forthcoming neurorehabilitation book chapter on mood assessment mentioned by the speaker.
  • The position paper on assessing mood and psychiatric issues after brain injury, described as under review.
  • Future publications concerning the formulation-based model, workflow, and manual.
  • Planned work developing new measures, including focus groups with people with lived experience of acquired brain injury.

  • Modern assessments should minimize sources of cultural variance and be usable across a broad range of groups rather than being developed for only one population.

Key Findings

  • An initial pilot or test-acceptability trial was conducted.

  • Regression-based norms were generated.
  • The team developed free software for analyzing MERS data.

Practical Or Clinical Implications

  • MERS may provide a more specific way to assess emotion-recognition abilities than clinical interview or observation alone.
  • Cross-cultural normative data and culturally considered test design may reduce the risk of invalid or unreliable interpretations.

11:50 Christian Salas Conceptual and Methodological Aspects in the Study of Emotional Reactivity Following Acquired Brain Injury in Adults: A Scoping Review Datablitz: Emerging tools for visual processing and emotionDatablitz DB11
Presenter: Christian Salas Programme time: Sunday 19 July 2026, 11:50 Note captured 12:04

Overview

The talk presented a systematic review of how emotional reactivity changes after brain injury have been conceptualized and assessed. The work was attributed to Braden Russes, a master's student collaborating on a larger project about changes in emotion regulation. The review aimed to identify dimensions of emotional reactivity and support development of better assessment and intervention models.

Main Arguments

  • Emotional reactivity is important because it is associated with psychiatric disorders, vulnerability to stress, loneliness, mental health changes, and community reintegration after brain injury.
  • Research in neuropsychology has used fragmented concepts and methods to describe emotional reactivity.
  • Terms such as irritability, apathy, emotional regulation, social recognition, emotional responses, and emotional reactivity may refer to overlapping but not identical phenomena.
  • Emotional reactivity should be treated as an umbrella construct rather than a single symptom.
  • Assessment should consider physiological responses, behavior, and subjective emotional experience, as well as the time course of responses.

Key Findings

  • The review identified 103 relevant papers, primarily involving traumatic brain injury and stroke, with samples collectively including more than 5,000 people with brain injury.
  • Approximately nine dimensions of emotional reactivity were identified through coding.
  • Of 40 papers that technically addressed emotional reactivity, only one specifically used the term emotional reactivity; most referred more generally to emotional responses.
  • Most studies addressed only one dimension of emotional reactivity and commonly focused on intensity or valence/balance.
  • Only 15% of papers assessed all three proposed components: physiological response, behavior, and subjective experience.
  • None of the reviewed studies used the highlighted time-course parameters of latency, rise time, persistence, and decay rate.
  • The speaker illustrated how different response profiles could theoretically distinguish apathy from hypomania or hyperemotionality and potentially inform rehabilitation.

Methods Or Evidence

  • The presenters conducted a systematic review using multiple keywords intended to capture different descriptions of emotional phenomena and emotional-reactivity changes.
  • The review included terms associated with processes such as intensity, rise time, persistence, and decay rate, along with clinical terms such as apathy and irritability.
  • Each of the 103 papers was thoroughly reviewed and coded for conceptual and measurement dimensions.
  • The talk did not provide detailed information about the search databases, inclusion and exclusion criteria, or formal quality-assessment procedures.

Practical Or Clinical Implications

  • Clinicians and researchers may need to distinguish among physiological, behavioral, and subjective aspects of emotional reactivity rather than relying on broad labels such as irritability or apathy.
  • Measuring the temporal course of emotional responses could provide more detailed profiles for assessment and rehabilitation planning.
  • More consistent terminology and multidimensional assessment may improve understanding of emotional changes after brain injury and support more targeted interventions.

Limitations And Uncertainties

  • The review found substantial conceptual and methodological inconsistency across the literature.
  • Most studies examined only fragments of emotional reactivity, limiting conclusions about the construct as a whole.
  • The evidence base was dominated by traumatic brain injury and stroke samples, so applicability to other forms of brain injury is uncertain.
  • The talk did not report quantitative meta-analytic results or establish which measurement approaches are most valid or clinically effective.
  • The proposed dimensions and theoretical patient profiles require further empirical validation.

  • No audience questions were included in the transcript.

Follow-Up Reading

  • No specific publications or additional reading materials were identified in the transcript.
  • Potential follow-up topics based on the talk include multidimensional models of emotion, temporal parameters of emotional responses, and assessment of emotional changes after traumatic brain injury and stroke.

13:45 Nicolò Zarotti & Katherine McIvor “It’s Knowing That There’s Other People and They’ve Accepted It”: Patients’ Experiences of an Acceptance and Commitment Therapy Group Intervention for People with Neurological Conditions Session 3: Mental wellbeing and adjustmentPlatform presentation
Presenter: Nicolò Zarotti & Katherine McIvor Programme time: Sunday 19 July 2026, 13:45 Note captured 14:03

Overview

The presentation described the development and service evaluation of an eight-session Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) group for people with neurological conditions. The group was designed to address adjustment challenges, improve timely access to psychological support, and provide peer support.

Main Arguments

  • Neurological conditions can involve reduced independence, dependence on others, stigma, misunderstanding, social withdrawal, uncertainty, and reduced control.
  • ACT was selected because it aims to increase psychological flexibility rather than challenge thoughts that may be realistic or accurate in the context of neurological illness.
  • The intervention focused on cognitive defusion or “unhooking,” acceptance, self-as-context, mindfulness, values, and committed action.
  • Group delivery was intended to provide an efficient, accessible form of psychological support and foster shared understanding and validation.

Key Findings

  • Participants initially reported barriers to attendance and engagement, including reluctance to share personal experiences and concerns about differences in participation styles.
  • A safe and accepting group atmosphere appeared to support trust, participation, sharing of lived experience, validation, and reduced feelings of loneliness and isolation.
  • Participants particularly described value-focused work and concrete value-based action, including the choice-point exercise, as useful ACT strategies.
  • The presenters concluded that ACT groups may be acceptable and meaningful for people with a broad range of neurological conditions and may improve access to psychological support.

Methods Or Evidence

  • This was a service evaluation of two iterations of the same ACT group, using the same materials and technology-delivery approach.
  • Across both groups, 13 patients attended; 10 agreed to post-group semi-structured interviews. The interview sample included six women and four men, with an average age of approximately 45 years and an age range of 25–61 years.
  • Half of the interview participants had brain tumours; other diagnoses included epilepsy, stroke, and chronic hydrocephalus.
  • Interviews were conducted by a person who was not involved in group delivery. The interview schedule drew on clinical expertise and relevant literature.
  • Data were analysed thematically using a primarily inductive approach, informed by Braun and Clarke’s guidance, within a critical realist epistemology.
  • The group comprised six weekly sessions, an individual review session after a two-week break, and a reunion session two months later. Delivery used a hybrid format, with some sessions face-to-face and others online.

Practical Or Clinical Implications

  • ACT groups may be a feasible way to provide psychological support to people with mixed neurological diagnoses, particularly where services face long waiting lists.
  • Hybrid delivery, written booklets, optional telephone catch-up, technology coaching, and between-session practice were used to support accessibility and carryover into daily life.
  • Group structure should balance opportunities for discussion with a clear agenda and should account for differences in participation, cognitive impairment, mobility or accessibility difficulties, and seizures.
  • Values clarification and small, concrete steps toward meaningful activities may be useful rehabilitation targets.

Limitations And Uncertainties

  • The evaluation was based on a small sample: 13 attendees and 10 interview participants, so the findings may not generalise broadly.
  • The transcript reports qualitative experiences but does not provide quantitative measures of psychological flexibility, symptoms, quality of life, waiting times, or clinical change.
  • There was no control or comparison group, and the findings cannot establish that ACT caused the reported benefits.
  • Interview participation was voluntary, so the perspectives of people who declined interviews or did not attend may be underrepresented.
  • The transcript does not specify the service setting in detail, the duration of each session, participant retention across sessions, or the exact diagnostic distribution.

Presenter: Ieke Winkens Programme time: Sunday 19 July 2026, 14:20 Note captured 14:20

Overview

The talk examined strategies for working with people with acquired brain injury who have impaired self-awareness of their deficits. It presented a three-level framework for tailoring professional interventions and reported findings from a step-wedge training study.

Main Arguments

  • Patients who do not recognize the relevance or value of rehabilitation may be less likely to engage with treatment.
  • The SETL3 framework categorizes patients as passerbys, searchers, or buyers according to their level of self-awareness and readiness to request or accept help.
  • Professional approaches should be tailored to these levels:
    • Passerby: build trust by connecting with the patient’s perspective, observing, asking open questions, and avoiding confrontation.
    • Searcher: help organize thoughts and clarify needs through psychoeducation and structured goal setting.
    • Buyer: focus on action and behavioral change through demonstration and joint practice.

Key Findings

  • Training professionals increased their perceived competence in working with patients with impaired self-awareness.
  • There was no significant overall interaction effect between time and intervention.
  • Three of four groups showed significant improvements in perceived competence during the intervention period.
  • Two groups showed improvements in patient self-awareness, although one group’s improvement had already begun during the control period.
  • Professionals understood the rationale and felt sufficiently skilled to use the strategies, but reported that the approach had not yet become part of their daily routine.

Methods Or Evidence

  • Participants were 67 professionals from Rehabilan, a Dutch organization providing specialized community-based support for people with acquired brain injury.
  • The professionals selected 80 clients with impaired self-awareness to participate.
  • The study used a step-wedge design, with four groups receiving the training sequentially at approximately 12-week intervals.
  • The training lasted two and a half days.
  • At multiple measurement points, professionals completed questionnaires concerning their competence and their clients’ self-awareness.

Practical Or Clinical Implications

  • Clinicians may benefit from assessing a patient’s level of self-awareness before selecting an intervention strategy.
  • Building a trusting relationship and avoiding confrontation may be particularly important for patients with very limited self-awareness.
  • Psychoeducation and structured goals may help patients who recognize that something has changed but cannot yet define their needs.
  • Demonstration and collaborative practice may be appropriate when patients recognize their need for support and are ready to act.
  • Additional implementation efforts may be needed to integrate the strategies into routine practice.

Limitations And Uncertainties

  • The training did not consistently improve patients’ self-awareness.
  • The study found no significant overall time-by-intervention interaction effect.
  • Improvement in one group had begun during the control period, making attribution to the intervention uncertain for that group.
  • The speaker suggested that the intervention may primarily change professionals’ approaches rather than directly increase patients’ self-awareness, and that more time may be needed for effects to emerge.
  • The transcript does not provide details about the measurement instruments, statistical effect sizes, or longer-term follow-up.

  • Eligibility included injury severity score greater than 8, being in paid or unpaid work before injury, capacity to consent, residence in the relevant catchment area, no planned retirement within 12 months, and no prior return to work.
  • The intervention began within 12 weeks of injury and continued for up to 12 months. Occupational therapists provided assessment, goal setting, job-focused rehabilitation, worksite assessment, employer liaison, workplace accommodations, and phased-return planning.
  • Participants were screened for anxiety, depression, and trauma-related distress using the GAD-7, PHQ-9, and Impact of Events scale. Those with severe scores could be referred to clinical psychology for interventions such as CBT or EMDR.
  • Follow-up questionnaires were collected at three, six, and 12 months. More than 80% of overall 12-month data were available.
  • Of the 710 participants, 91% started the intervention. Occupational therapy involved an average of eight sessions over six months; 41% were referred to clinical psychology. Only 39% consented to employer contact.

Practical Or Clinical Implications

  • A structured OT and clinical psychology package should not be assumed to improve 12-month return-to-work outcomes beyond usual NHS rehabilitation for a broadly selected major-trauma population.
  • Employment context matters: the trial may have included many participants who were already supported by stable employment, large employers, permanent contracts, and the ability to self-advocate.
  • Future services may need to focus more specifically on people with greater injury severity, less secure employment, fewer workplace resources, or greater social disadvantage.
  • The intervention was reportedly highly valued by participants despite the absence of significant improvements in the measured outcomes.
  • The speaker noted that the COVID-19 pandemic shifted delivery toward remote care and may have facilitated employment for some white-collar workers through hybrid working.

Limitations And Uncertainties

  • The intervention was delivered during a period affected by the COVID-19 pandemic, which may have influenced both intervention delivery and employment patterns.
  • The study population was not representative of all people with major trauma, particularly those who were more socially deprived, less highly educated, in less secure employment, or from minority ethnic groups.
  • Most participants had fractures; only 74 had traumatic brain injury and 16 had spinal injury, limiting conclusions for these groups.
  • Employer involvement was limited because only 39% of participants consented to contact with their employer.
  • The trial was powered on an assumed 15-percentage-point benefit and an expected 60% return-to-work rate in usual care; the observed findings did not support that effect.
  • The speaker indicated that longer-term effects remain uncertain and that a funded five-year follow-up study will examine outcomes at approximately two and four to five years after injury.

17:00 Sue Sloan Enhancing allied health capacity to improve activity, participation and behaviour outcomes after extremely severe acquired brain injury: A qualitative study of experienced Allied Health Practitioners Session 9: Community based rehabilitationPlatform presentation
Presenter: Sue Sloan Programme time: Monday 20 July 2026, 17:00 Note captured 17:19

Overview

The talk presented findings from interviews with 23 experienced allied health practitioners supporting adults with extremely severe acquired or traumatic brain injury, complex daily support needs, and challenging situations. The study examined how practitioners work to improve meaningful participation and address behaviour.

Main Arguments

  • Behaviour and participation are bidirectionally related and should not be treated as separate clinical domains.
  • Restricting participation can increase boredom, stress, and social isolation, contributing to a downward spiral of more challenging situations and less participation.
  • Providing meaningful participation despite ongoing challenging situations can support skill development, pro-social behaviour, improved mood, and an upward spiral toward greater participation.
  • Effective practice is relationship-based, interdisciplinary, embedded in everyday routines, and dependent on a well-supported paid workforce.
  • The speaker advocated using the term “challenging situation” rather than “challenging behaviour” to shift attention toward environmental, activity, and support contexts.

Key Findings

  • The interviewed practitioners described nine interrelated themes: building relationships; understanding acquired brain injury, behaviour change, and comorbidities; working in teams; conducting functional assessments; integrating behaviour, activity, and participation interventions; treating behaviour as everyone’s responsibility; constructing supportive environments; working with disability support workers; and pursuing positive relationships among participation, behaviour, and mental health.
  • The 23 clients were all classified in Care and Needs Scale group A, indicating complex care needs, with reported support requirements of approximately 20–24 hours per day, often using a two-to-one staffing ratio.
  • Clients displayed an average of eight domains of challenging behaviour on the measure described as the Inverted Behaviour Scale.
  • Practitioners reported working with clients for an average of six years and viewed their roles as ongoing.
  • Meaningful participation was consistently linked by practitioners with improved mood, while improved mood was linked with reductions in an important antecedent to challenging situations.

Methods Or Evidence

  • The study used semi-structured interviews to elicit the tacit, practice-based knowledge of experienced allied health practitioners.
  • Participants were registered or credentialed practitioners with at least five years of experience, currently working in an Australian community setting. They came from four Australian states and had an average of 16 years of relevant community experience.
  • Each practitioner nominated a current adult client with challenging behaviour, daily support needs, more than 12 months of therapeutic involvement, and at least one successful outcome.
  • Interviews lasted approximately 90 minutes. Practitioners used examples from their work with the nominated client.
  • The talk situated the study within a literature review indicating that positive behaviour support may reduce challenging behaviour in the short term, but that findings are mixed, follow-up is often short, people with significant comorbidities are frequently excluded, and meaningful participation is often not measured.

Practical Or Clinical Implications

  • Assess and address behaviour and participation together rather than in separate systems or silos.
  • Anchor interventions to the person’s meaningful activities, valued roles, and goals.
  • Integrate behavioural, activity, and participation strategies into everyday routines.
  • Use interdisciplinary teamwork involving allied health professionals, family members, and paid support workers.
  • Provide disability support workers with instruction, role modelling, reflective practice, and ongoing guidance because they implement much of the daily program.
  • Design environments and routines that reduce triggers, support purposeful activity, foster social relationships, and enable enjoyment.
  • Adopt a long-term perspective; change may depend on sustained relationships and ongoing support.

Limitations And Uncertainties

  • The study intentionally recruited practitioners who could describe successful outcomes, so the findings may not represent unsuccessful interventions or the full range of clinical experiences.
  • The evidence was based on practitioner accounts and tacit knowledge rather than direct observation or controlled outcome comparisons.
  • The sample included 23 practitioner–client cases from Australian community settings, limiting generalisability to other populations, settings, or health systems.
  • The talk did not report client self-report data, direct participation measures, or quantitative tests of the proposed bidirectional relationship.
  • The speaker noted that prior positive behaviour support research has often used short follow-up periods and has not consistently measured meaningful participation; the current interview study does not by itself resolve those evidence gaps.
