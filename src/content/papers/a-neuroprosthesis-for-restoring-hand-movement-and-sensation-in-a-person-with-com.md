## Summary
This study reports a first-in-human “double neural bypass” system designed to help restore both movement and sensation after complete cervical spinal cord injury. The setup combines an intracortical brain-computer interface, spinal cord stimulation, and cortical microstimulation in a single hybrid neuroprosthetic platform. The participant was a 42-year-old man with chronic complete C4 sensory/C5 motor tetraplegia. Before testing the full system, the researchers established stable baselines and evaluated transcutaneous spinal cord stimulation (tSCS) paired with activity-based training. tSCS alone was used to target cervical spinal roots and to support elbow and hand-related training. In this therapeutic phase, the participant showed persistent improvements in voluntary elbow flexion force over weeks to months. Those gains were bilateral and translated into a practical improvement in being able to bring both hands to his face. However, tSCS alone did not restore useful hand strength or tactile sensation in the most affected distal regions. To address that gap, the team implemented the assistive neuroprosthetic mode using recordings from motor cortex to decode intended hand movements. Decoding relied on an LSTM-based neural network trained on intracortical activity, and the output drove neuromuscular electrical stimulation of the participant’s own hand. Sensory feedback was added through intracortical microstimulation in somatosensory cortex, with force sensors on the hand used to evoke tactile percepts during grasp. With this closed-loop setup, the participant could perform functional hand actions such as opening and closing the hand, manipulating objects, and self-feeding. The paper also reports that the system produced clinically relevant sensory feedback and that some improvements, such as better wrist tactile sensation and elbow function, persisted even when the system was off.
## Why This Matters
This work shows that brain-to-body neuroprosthetics may do more than temporarily assist movement; they may also help drive longer-lasting recovery when paired with targeted stimulation. For people with severe paralysis, that combination of immediate control and possible rehabilitation effect is especially important.
## Caveats and Limitations
This is a single-participant study, so the findings cannot yet be assumed to generalize to other people with spinal cord injury. The results also reflect a complex, highly personalized system with multiple stimulation and decoding components, which makes it hard to isolate which part drove each benefit. Longer-term durability, scalability, and broader clinical validation still need to be tested in larger cohorts.
## Key Takeaways
- A hybrid ‘double neural bypass’ linked brain decoding, spinal stimulation, and sensory cortex stimulation.
- tSCS paired with training improved elbow flexion and produced lasting functional gains.
- Motor cortex decoding controlled electrical stimulation of the participant’s own hand.
- Somatosensory cortex microstimulation provided tactile feedback during grasping tasks.
- The study is promising but is still based on a single-person first-in-human case.
