## Summary
This paper asks how the cellular activity of human cortex represents the structure of language during natural speech. The authors recorded single-neuron activity across frontotemporal language regions in eight neurosurgical participants and aligned those spikes to words in self-generated sentences.

They then used language-model and parsing tools to label the produced words with grammatical, syntactic and semantic features. Individual neurons responded to features such as parts of speech, dependency relationships, constituency depth, phrase boundaries and sentence context. At the population level, those activity patterns could decode several linguistic features above chance.

The result is a multi-scale view of language encoding: single neurons carry fine-grained linguistic information, local populations organize those representations, and the strongest encoding is distributed across left-lateralized frontotemporal regions.

## Why This Matters
Language neuroscience often works at the scale of regions, networks or intracranial field potentials. This study gets closer to the neural units that actually compute with linguistic structure. It suggests that grammatical and semantic information is represented at a surprisingly granular cellular level during speech production.

## Caveats and Limitations
- The sample is necessarily small and clinical, with electrode placement determined by medical care.
- Coverage is broad for implanted regions but not a complete map of the language network.
- NLP-derived labels make the analysis tractable, but they are still model-dependent descriptions of linguistic structure.
- The study focuses on English language production, not comprehension, reading or multilingual language use.

## Key Takeaways
- Human frontotemporal neurons encode multiple levels of linguistic structure.
- Some neurons track local word properties, while others reflect broader sentence organization.
- Linguistic information is represented beyond what broader field-potential signals capture.
- The strongest language encoding was left-lateralized and regionally varied.
