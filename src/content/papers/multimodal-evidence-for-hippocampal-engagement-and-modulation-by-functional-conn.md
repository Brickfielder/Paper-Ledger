## Summary
This study tested whether transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) targeted with hippocampal functional connectivity can directly engage the human hippocampus. The authors combined two complementary approaches: intracranial EEG in 8 neurosurgical patients and fMRI in 79 neurologically healthy participants. In both settings, TMS was delivered to individualized parietal sites chosen for their functional connectivity with the hippocampus. The main question was whether this FC-guided targeting produces measurable hippocampal responses rather than only broad cortical effects. With single-pulse TMS, the investigators found hippocampal activity changes with distinct temporal and spectral signatures. These evoked responses were preferentially observed when stimulation was directed to the individualized parietal target rather than being treated as a generic cortical perturbation. Response variability across individuals tracked the strength of parietal-hippocampal functional connectivity, which supports the targeting strategy. The paper also used repetitive TMS to ask whether repeated stimulation can modulate hippocampal oscillations over a longer timescale. In that condition, stimulation to the FC-guided parietal site selectively suppressed hippocampal theta activity. Together, the intracranial and fMRI results provide converging evidence that the parietal target can access a hippocampal network through connectivity-based personalization. The study therefore moves beyond correlational imaging by showing causal modulation of a deep memory-related structure from the cortex. A key methodological strength is the multimodal design, because invasive electrophysiology and noninvasive imaging each address different aspects of target engagement. A major limitation is that the direct neural evidence comes from a small clinical iEEG sample, so generalization to the broader population remains uncertain.
## Why This Matters
This paper supports a practical route for personalized brain stimulation: use each person’s own connectivity map to pick a cortical target that reaches the hippocampus. That matters because the hippocampus is important for memory, yet it is difficult to stimulate directly without invasive procedures.
## Caveats and Limitations
The strongest direct evidence comes from only 8 neurosurgical patients, which limits statistical power and generalizability. The study shows engagement and oscillatory suppression, but it does not by itself establish durable cognitive benefit or prove that theta suppression is the mechanism of memory improvement in typical clinical use.
## Key Takeaways
- FC-guided parietal TMS can evoke measurable hippocampal responses.
- Individual differences in response tracked parietal-hippocampal connectivity strength.
- Repetitive stimulation selectively suppressed hippocampal theta oscillations.
- Intracranial EEG and fMRI together provided converging causal evidence.
- The clinical sample was small, so broader validation is still needed.
