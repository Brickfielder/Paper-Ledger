## Summary
This Cell study reports a high-resolution spatial map of olfactory receptor neuron identities in mouse nasal epithelium and links that map to wiring logic in the olfactory bulb. Instead of near-random receptor placement within broad zones, the authors describe a reproducible spatial code in which local position biases receptor choice and helps pre-align peripheral receptor organization with central sensory maps. The work combines large-scale spatial transcriptomics, genetic perturbations, and circuit-level mapping to argue that developmental patterning cues constrain receptor identity before long-range axon targeting fully matures.

## Why This Matters
Olfaction has lagged behind vision and audition in mechanistic map-level understanding. This paper offers a concrete developmental principle that could connect receptor identity, tissue patterning, and brain map construction in a single framework.

## Caveats and Limitations
The strongest evidence appears to come from mouse models and developmental perturbations, so species generalization (especially to humans) remains open. As with many spatial-omics-heavy studies, causal resolution is strongest for tested pathways but not exhaustive across all candidate regulators.

## Key Takeaways
- Olfactory receptor choice appears spatially structured at finer resolution than older zonal models implied.
- Peripheral developmental patterning may preconfigure aspects of central olfactory map alignment.
- The paper integrates atlas-scale measurements with perturbation experiments, strengthening mechanistic interpretation.
- This framework may help future restoration strategies for smell disorders, but translational relevance still needs direct human validation.
