## Summary

Lee and colleagues use next-generation ultra-high-gradient diffusion MRI to ask whether cortical laminar architecture can be measured noninvasively in living humans. The study leverages a Connectome MRI scanner with very high gradient strength and slew rate, then applies super-resolution processing to produce 1 mm diffusion MRI suitable for cortical depth analysis.

The key modelling tool is SANDI, which estimates signal fractions associated with soma and neurite compartments. Across cortical depth, the intra-soma signal fraction peaks around the middle of the cortex, while intra-neurite signal increases toward deeper layers. Those profiles are broadly consistent with known histological patterns.

The paper also reports region-specific and geometry-specific effects. The visual cortex shows higher intra-soma signal than motor cortex, especially in deeper layers, and intra-soma signal relates differently to cortical curvature depending on depth. Together, these results suggest that diffusion MRI can begin to capture laminar microstructure rather than only coarse tissue contrast.

## Why This Matters

Human laminar neuroscience is usually limited by the need for histology or by the coarse resolution of noninvasive imaging. This work pushes diffusion MRI toward an in vivo surrogate for cortical cytoarchitecture and myeloarchitecture. That could eventually support studies of cortical development, ageing, disease, and functional specialization at a finer anatomical scale.

## Caveats and Limitations

The hardware is a central part of the result. Ultra-high-gradient systems are not widely available, and the analysis depends on model assumptions, super-resolution reconstruction, and careful cortical depth sampling. The paper demonstrates feasibility, but routine use will require replication, validation against histology, and assessment on more typical MRI systems.

## Key Takeaways

- Ultra-high-gradient diffusion MRI can probe cortical depth-dependent microstructure in vivo.
- SANDI metrics show laminar profiles consistent with histological expectations.
- Soma-related signal peaks around mid-cortical depth; neurite-related signal increases deeper.
- Visual and motor cortex differ in laminar microstructural profiles.
- The approach is promising but currently hardware- and model-dependent.
