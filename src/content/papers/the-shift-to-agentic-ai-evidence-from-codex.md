## Summary

This OpenAI paper analyzes usage data from Codex to study how agentic AI changes
work. The authors contrast Codex with conversational AI interfaces: ChatGPT is
treated mainly as a conversational tool, while Codex is treated as an agentic
tool that can inspect files, use tools, execute commands and create or modify
artifacts on a user's behalf.

The analysis compares three populations: individual personal-account users,
external organizational users and OpenAI workers. The paper reports rapid but
uneven adoption. Codex weekly active users increased more than fivefold in the
first half of 2026. Individual-user adoption remained relatively narrow, while
organizational adoption was broader and OpenAI internal use was nearly
universal.

The paper's main conceptual point is that agentic AI use is delegated
production, not just advice-seeking. Codex requests include debugging,
refactoring, validation, application configuration, document drafting and data
analysis. Users increasingly delegate longer tasks, including tasks estimated to
take an experienced human more than eight hours.

The strongest usage shift appears inside OpenAI. By June 11, 2026, Codex
accounted for 99.8% of output tokens generated across Codex and ChatGPT by
OpenAI workers. Among organizational users the corresponding share was 63.3%,
while among individual users it was 16.5%. The paper also reports that
non-developer use grew quickly, especially in settings where adoption was
deepest.

The paper then looks at how intensive users organize work. Measures include
output volume, task complexity, skill use, runtime and concurrency. More than
10% of users managed three or more concurrent Codex agents at some point each
week, and 26.6% used skills. The authors interpret this as evidence that heavy
users treat Codex less like a chat assistant and more like a workflow system for
delegating, monitoring and coordinating multiple streams of work.

## Why This Matters

The paper is valuable because it studies real usage rather than only benchmark
capability. It shows that the important margin for agentic AI is not simply
whether someone opens a tool, but what work they delegate and how much execution
the system performs.

It also makes the organizational point clear. Adoption varies sharply across
individuals, companies and OpenAI itself, suggesting that workflow design,
permissions, skill sharing, supervision norms and review processes matter as
much as raw model capability.

## Caveats and Limitations

The paper is descriptive. It does not directly estimate productivity from a
randomized intervention, and output tokens are a proxy for AI-mediated work, not
verified useful output. More tokens can reflect more work, longer workflows, or
different user behavior.

OpenAI workers are also not representative of typical organizations. They have
high model familiarity, strong organizational support, low marginal usage cost
and unusually close contact with the product being studied. The external usage
patterns are therefore more conservative but also more heterogeneous.

## Key Takeaways

- Codex active users grew more than fivefold in early 2026.
- Agentic AI usage is framed as delegated production rather than conversation.
- Codex adoption is deepest inside OpenAI, broader in organizations than among
  individual users.
- Heavy users increasingly run parallel agents, use skills and delegate longer
  tasks.
- The paper shows adoption and workflow change, not causal productivity effects.
