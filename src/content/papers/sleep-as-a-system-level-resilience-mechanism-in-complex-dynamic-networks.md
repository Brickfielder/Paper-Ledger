## Summary
This perspective argues that sleep is best understood not only as rest, memory support or metabolic cleanup, but as a system-level resilience process for the brain.

The authors bring together several familiar sleep functions: synaptic renormalization, replay, glymphatic clearance, stage-structured oscillations and changes in large-scale connectivity. Their claim is that these are coordinated parts of a broader maintenance strategy. Sleep helps the brain recover from waking perturbations, avoid overly specialized network states and preserve the capacity for flexible information processing.

The paper also draws a comparison with artificial neural networks. Offline phases, replay-like training and regularization can help machine systems balance stability and plasticity during continual learning. The analogy is not that artificial systems literally sleep, but that both biological and artificial networks may need structured downtime to remain adaptable without forgetting or destabilizing.

## Why This Matters
Sleep research can become fragmented across memory, waste clearance, immune function and psychiatric symptoms. A resilience framing puts those pieces into one network-level account. It is also useful for AI, where catastrophic forgetting and brittle adaptation are practical problems rather than metaphors.

## Caveats and Limitations
- The article is a perspective, so it synthesizes and reframes existing work rather than reporting a new experiment.
- The biological-to-artificial comparison is conceptually productive but not mechanistically identical.
- Clinical implications for insomnia, neurodegeneration and affective symptoms remain hypotheses that need targeted tests.

## Key Takeaways
- Sleep may maintain brain resilience by restoring large-scale network dynamics.
- Synaptic homeostasis, replay and clearance can be read as coordinated maintenance processes.
- Sleep disruption may matter clinically because it reduces recoverability, not only because it causes fatigue.
- Offline phases in artificial neural networks offer a useful comparison for continual learning and stability.
