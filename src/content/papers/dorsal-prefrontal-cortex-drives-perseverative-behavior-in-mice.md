## Summary
The paper asks what brain circuits support perseveration, the tendency to repeat a recent action even when another choice would yield a better reward. The authors trained head-fixed mice on a probabilistic reversal-learning task in which the rewarded choice switched across blocks. In this setting, mice often kept choosing the previously rewarded side after the contingency changed, so their behavior was strongly influenced by past choices. The mice were also faster when repeating the same action than when switching actions. To characterize behavior, the authors compared several trial-history models and found that the best-fitting ones all included a perseverative component. They chose a simple perseveration/reward-learning model that separates choice repetition from reward-based updating. In that model, the perseverative term had a larger influence than the reward-learning term in every mouse they tested. The authors then recorded spiking activity with Neuropixels probes across frontal areas during task performance. Neurons in anterior secondary motor cortex, or MOs, showed activity correlated with the perseveration variable, including during the fixation period before movement was allowed. These correlations were much weaker or absent in other frontal regions. The same MOs activity also predicted faster reaction times, linking the neural signal to both choice repetition and speed. When the authors optogenetically inhibited anterior MOs during the choice period, perseveration dropped and reactions slowed. In contrast, inhibiting medial prefrontal cortex during choice did not alter behavior, although inhibiting it after reward delivery impaired learning over multiple trials. Overall, the results suggest that anterior MOs carries a perseverative decision variable and is required for using that variable to shape choice and reaction time in this task.
## Why This Matters
This study helps separate two components of decision-making that are often mixed together: learning from reward and simply repeating what was just done. It points to a specific frontal brain area that seems to support action repetition in mice, which could help explain why habits and repeated choices can be so sticky.
## Caveats and Limitations
The findings come from one head-fixed reversal-learning task in mice, so it is not yet clear how broadly they generalize to other behaviors, species, or more naturalistic settings. The model results show strong associations, but they do not prove that perseveration is a single causal computation rather than a task-specific mix of strategies. The optogenetic effects are informative, but they also reflect acute disruption of a circuit, which may not capture how the same region normally operates across longer timescales.
## Key Takeaways
- Mice in this task relied heavily on previous choices, not just reward history.
- A simple model with separate perseveration and reward-learning terms fit behavior well.
- Anterior secondary motor cortex (MOs) contained neural signals related to perseveration.
- Silencing MOs during choice reduced perseveration and slowed responses.
- Medial prefrontal cortex seemed more important for post-reward learning than for choice repetition.
