## Summary
This paper studies a specific kind of positivity: a tendency to accept options more readily when decisions require integrating gains and losses. The authors ask whether that **positive choice bias** is related to psychological resilience, and whether the link can be explained by cognitive-computational and neural mechanisms.

Participants completed a cost-benefit integration task. Each compound visual stimulus had features linked to gains and losses, and participants had to decide whether to accept or reject the option. The authors measured behaviour, fitted a linear ballistic accumulator model to decision processes and recorded fMRI during the task. Resilience was measured with the RS-25, with later analyses focusing on the trait acceptance subscale.

## Main Findings
The behavioural result is focused rather than broad. Positive choice bias showed weak evidence of association with the overall resilience score, but stronger evidence for association with **trait acceptance**, a facet involving equanimity, purpose and low worry.

The modelling result suggests that positive choice bias was not mainly an "accept by default" tendency. Instead, it was associated with drift-rate-related parameters: lower valuation of losses and lower sensitivity to smaller negative values relative to gains. Put simply, people with a more positive choice bias appeared less pulled around by negative information during the decision process.

The fMRI result adds a regulatory layer. Choice bias and trait acceptance overlapped in their associations with loss-related activation across prefrontal and parietal regions, including dorsal anterior cingulate cortex, dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, inferior frontal junction, frontal eye fields, anterior middle frontal gyrus, posterior superior frontal gyrus, anterior insula and intraparietal sulcus. Participants with more positive choice bias and higher trait acceptance showed stronger increases in activity in these regions when processing higher loss information.

The authors also found an overlapping negative association for gain-related activity in the right inferior frontal junction. Across the overlap regions, loss- and gain-related activity statistically mediated the cross-sectional association between choice bias and trait acceptance.

Connectivity analyses were more exploratory. Some associations linked choice bias or trait acceptance to connectivity between prefrontal seeds and subcortical or valuation-related regions, including midbrain, striatum and ventromedial prefrontal cortex. However, the overlap between choice-bias and trait-acceptance connectivity effects was limited, and mediation was not supported for those connectivity regions.

## Why This Matters
The paper is useful because it treats resilience as something that may be partly built from valuation and control processes during everyday decisions. A resilient style is not framed as ignoring negative information. In fact, people with more positive choice bias and trait acceptance showed stronger activity in control-related regions when negative information was present.

That pattern supports a more nuanced account: positive bias may depend on regulating or reweighting negative information rather than simply failing to perceive it.

## Caveats
- The study is cross-sectional, so the mediation analyses are statistical rather than causal.
- Trait resilience is measured by questionnaire, not by longitudinal adaptation after stress exposure.
- The strongest resilience link was with trait acceptance, not the full RS-25 score.
- The authors note that brain-behaviour correlations were smaller than assumed for power planning and require replication in larger samples.
- The manuscript is a pre-publication accepted copy; the preprint DOI is available, but the final journal DOI was not in the local PDF.

## Bottom Line
This is a careful model-based fMRI study linking positive choice bias to trait acceptance and to neural regulation of value information. Its most interesting claim is that resilience-related positivity may involve stronger control-region engagement when processing negative information, alongside reduced computational weighting of losses during decision making.
