## Summary

The study asks which neurons recruited during associative learning become the
core of a memory engram. The authors used f-FLiCRE, a fast variant of FLiCRE
that labels cells only when light delivery coincides with high intracellular
calcium. This gave them short tagging windows within mouse contextual fear
conditioning rather than the minutes-to-hours windows typical of
immediate-early-gene tagging.

They tagged dorsal CA1 neurons during four acquisition periods: preshock,
shock, freezing and nonfreezing. Optogenetic reactivation in a neutral context
triggered freezing only for shock- and freezing-tagged ensembles. The same two
ensembles were also necessary: inhibiting them during later recall reduced
freezing, whereas inhibiting preshock or nonfreezing ensembles did not.

Calcium imaging supported this functional dissociation. The acquisition
subensembles were largely nonoverlapping, and shock- and freezing-related cells
showed distinct recall dynamics. By contrast, cells active during freezing and
nonfreezing periods at recall both contributed to subsequent expression,
suggesting that the selective recruitment rule is strongest during learning.

## Why This Matters

The paper resolves a common ambiguity in engram experiments: cells active
during one experience are not interchangeable simply because they were active
near the same time. The informational and behavioural context of their activity
helps determine whether they become a causal part of the later memory trace.

## Caveats and Limitations

- The findings concern one hippocampal region, one aversive conditioning task and mice.
- The tagging windows are experimenter-defined, so alternative temporal partitions could reveal further subensembles.
- Optogenetic sufficiency and necessity do not specify the natural synaptic code or downstream circuit mechanism.
- The relation between fear-memory ensembles and other episodic or declarative memories remains untested.

## Key Takeaways

- High-temporal-precision tagging separates CA1 cells active during different learning moments.
- Shock- and freezing-active acquisition ensembles can independently drive fear recall.
- Those same ensembles are necessary for normal recall, unlike preshock and nonfreezing ensembles.
- Engram recruitment depends on when and under what conditions a neuron is active during learning.
