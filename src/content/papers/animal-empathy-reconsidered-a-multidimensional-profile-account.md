## Summary

The authors argue that asking whether a species "has empathy" forces a complex
capacity into a yes-or-no category. They instead propose a multidimensional
profile account designed for comparisons within and between species.

The framework scores five dimensions: registration of another individual's
affective state, registration of their situation, registration of their
cognitive states, behavioural and cognitive flexibility, and social
coordination. Each dimension is linked to observable features and experimental
paradigms, allowing researchers to describe both the presence and quality of
empathetic behaviour without assuming that every species must approximate the
human pattern.

The review applies this account to rodents, great apes, canids and corvids. The
result is a set of distinct empathy profiles with family resemblances rather
than a linear hierarchy. Rats, for example, show meaningful emotion- and
situation-sensitive helping with strong flexibility, while showing much less
evidence of sensitivity to another's broader mental state.

## Why This Matters

Comparative cognition often oscillates between anthropomorphism and excessive
scepticism. A profile approach preserves genuine cross-species similarities
while making differences explicit, and it turns broad labels into testable
questions about component abilities.

## Caveats and Limitations

- Available evidence varies greatly in quantity, paradigm and reliability across species.
- Profile dimensions simplify capacities that may interact rather than operate independently.
- Behavioural evidence cannot by itself establish shared subjective experience or mechanism.
- The framework's predictive value needs prospective testing beyond the reviewed examples.

## Key Takeaways

- Empathy is better represented as a multidimensional profile than a binary trait.
- Five dimensions capture perception of others, flexibility and coordinated response.
- Rodents, apes, canids and corvids show different but partly overlapping profiles.
- Similar behaviour may reflect different cognitive capacities in different species.
