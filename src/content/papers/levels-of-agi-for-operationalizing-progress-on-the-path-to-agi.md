## Summary
This paper is a position piece that proposes a framework for classifying artificial general intelligence and systems that may precede it. The authors argue that the field needs a shared, operational way to talk about AGI so that researchers, policymakers, and deployers can compare systems more consistently. To motivate the framework, they review several influential AGI-related definitions and stress-test them against a set of six principles for a useful ontology. A central claim is that AGI should be defined in terms of capabilities rather than internal processes such as consciousness, human-like thinking, or specific brain-inspired mechanisms. The paper also argues that both generality and performance matter, rather than either one alone. Based on this view, the authors propose “Levels of AGI” as a two-dimensional taxonomy organized around depth of performance and breadth of generality. They emphasize that a system can be broad but unreliable, or strong on a narrow set of tasks without being generally capable. The article also discusses how autonomy and deployment context affect the risk profile of highly capable systems. Another important point is that benchmarks for AGI should be able to measure behavior and capabilities in a way that is robust to superficial task success. The authors highlight the difficulty of building benchmarks that are both comprehensive and truly diagnostic of AGI-level competence. They also note that some candidate definitions, such as Turing-test style approaches or purely economic definitions, capture only part of what people often mean by AGI. The paper further argues that the choice of human-AI interaction paradigm matters for responsible deployment. Overall, the main contribution is a conceptual framework meant to make progress on AGI more measurable and discussable, rather than a new empirical result. Because it is a position paper, its value lies in synthesis and framing rather than experimental validation.
## Why This Matters
This paper matters because people often use “AGI” to mean different things, which makes research claims, safety discussions, and policy debates hard to compare. A clearer framework can help separate hype from measurable progress and make it easier to talk about what systems can actually do.
## Caveats and Limitations
The paper is conceptual rather than experimental, so it does not validate the proposed levels against real benchmark data. Its framework also depends on judgment calls about what counts as generality, performance, and relevant tasks, which may be contested. In addition, any operational definition of AGI will likely age quickly as models and benchmarks change.
## Key Takeaways
- AGI should be defined by capabilities, not by internal processes like consciousness or human-like thought.
- Generality and performance are both necessary dimensions; broadness alone is not enough.
- The proposed “Levels of AGI” framework is meant to help compare systems and track progress.
- Benchmarks for AGI need to be robust, comprehensive, and able to distinguish superficial success from real competence.
- Deployment and autonomy matter, because the same capabilities can imply different risks in different settings.
