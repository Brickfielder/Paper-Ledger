## Summary
This review argues that perceptual decision-making is better understood as a distributed process across many brain areas than as the output of a single central executive. The authors contrast older feedforward, modular models with newer evidence for recurrent, network-level computations. They focus on how large-scale recordings, task designs that separate different components of decision-making, causal perturbations, and population-level analyses are changing the field. A central example comes from brain-wide Neuropixels recordings in mice performing a standardized visual decision task. Those recordings showed that different task variables had different spatial and temporal signatures across the brain. Sensory signals were strongest and earliest in visual cortex and related thalamic regions. Choice signals emerged more gradually and were widespread across cortical and subcortical areas. Movement and reward-related signals were even more broadly distributed. Prior expectations also shifted baseline activity and choice representations across multiple regions, consistent with loop-like interactions rather than a single site that adds prior information at the end. The authors interpret these results as evidence for a modular but distributed organization, not a uniform “everything is everywhere” pattern. They then discuss primate work showing how, in a slow dynamic reaching task, evidence, urgency, and commitment unfold across premotor cortex, motor cortex, basal ganglia, and prefrontal cortex. In that work, evidence tracking during deliberation differs from the signals that appear around commitment, supporting a biased competition view rather than a one-shot decision center. The review also emphasizes task designs that decouple sensory judgments from motor plans, because standard tasks often let decision formation and action planning evolve together. Overall, the paper proposes a modern framework in which decisions emerge from interacting population dynamics, not from a centralized executive module.
## Why This Matters
This matters because it changes how researchers should look for the neural basis of choice. Instead of searching for one decision-making hub, the paper argues for studying how multiple areas coordinate, influence one another, and separate perception from action.
## Caveats and Limitations
This is a review, so it synthesizes evidence rather than presenting a new primary dataset. Much of the argument depends on specific tasks, species, and recording regimes, so generalization to other behaviors or to humans remains uncertain. The distributed signals discussed here are informative, but they do not by themselves prove causal roles for every area that shows decision-related activity.
## Key Takeaways
- Decision-making appears to emerge from distributed, recurrent brain dynamics rather than a single central executive.
- Brain-wide recordings show that stimulus, choice, movement, reward, and prior signals have different spatial and temporal profiles.
- Prior expectations seem to influence many regions through network loops, not just one specialized decision area.
- In primate tasks, evidence, urgency, and commitment can be separated across prefrontal, premotor, motor, and basal ganglia circuits.
- Task design and causal manipulation are key for distinguishing perceptual processing from motor planning.
