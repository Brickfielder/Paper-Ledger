## Summary

This Science Advances paper presents SensoExo, a portable assistive system for
people with impaired hand function after central neurological injury. The device
combines a hand exoskeleton with a custom NeuroSleeve that delivers targeted
transcutaneous electrical stimulation.

The system has two main stimulation roles. TENS is used to provide tactile
feedback by stimulating median and ulnar nerve pathways, with fingertip force
sensors modulating stimulation during grasp. FES is used to stimulate flexor and
extensor muscles, assisting hand opening and closing. The exoskeleton provides
mechanical support, while the sleeve adds movement assistance and sensory
information.

The authors designed the sleeve with data-informed electrode placement and
tested the integrated system in 14 participants with stroke, spinal cord injury
or brain injury. The setup was adapted to user abilities, including control from
residual finger movement or manual switches when residual movement was absent.
The system was battery powered, backpack-mounted and designed for real-time
operation.

Across participants, SensoExo restored or improved finger mobility and produced
functionally meaningful tactile percepts, including in some people with
clinically complete sensory loss. In the functional subset of eight
participants, neurostimulation improved grasp-related performance by supporting
precision, grip force or manipulation of difficult objects.

## Why This Matters

Hand function is hard to restore because useful grasp depends on both motor
output and sensory feedback. Many exoskeletons help movement but do not restore
touch, while stimulation garments may not provide enough mechanical assistance.
SensoExo tries to merge both routes in a wearable system.

The most interesting translational point is the use of residual peripheral
pathways after central injury. If noninvasive stimulation can recover useful
touch cues and reinforce grasp in daily tasks, wearable devices may become more
useful before fully implanted neural interfaces are clinically routine.

## Caveats and Limitations

The participant group was small and clinically heterogeneous, which is sensible
for early feasibility work but limits broad inference. Only eight participants
completed the functional assessments, and the paper does not yet show long-term
independent home use.

The system is also personalized. That is clinically appropriate, but it means
deployment will depend on calibration time, donning burden, comfort, durability
and whether users keep using the device outside supervised sessions.

## Key Takeaways

- SensoExo combines a hand exoskeleton with targeted noninvasive stimulation.
- TENS supplied somatotopic tactile feedback from fingertip force sensors.
- FES assisted finger flexion and extension.
- The system was tested in 14 people with central neurological injuries.
- Functional testing in eight participants showed improved grasp precision or
  strength in selected tasks.
