## Summary

This Nature Human Behaviour paper asks whether neural similarity among friends
is only the result of friendship, or whether it can exist before people become
friends. The authors use a longitudinal design in an incoming graduate-student
cohort. Before students had much chance to meet, 41 of them underwent fMRI while
watching naturalistic movie clips. The full 288-person cohort then completed
social-network surveys two months and eight months later.

The design lets the authors separate pre-existing neural similarity from later
social proximity. They compute inter-participant neural similarity across brain
regions while students watched the same movies, then ask whether those patterns
predict friendship distance in the later network. Future friends did not differ
from all non-friends collapsed together, but they did show higher pre-existing
similarity in left orbitofrontal cortex than dyads who ended up three degrees
apart.

The stronger result comes from change over time. Dyads who grew socially closer
between the two-month and eight-month surveys had greater pre-existing neural
similarity than dyads who drifted apart. This appeared across a broader set of
regions, including bilateral orbitofrontal cortex and regions spanning the
default-mode network, frontoparietal control network, dorsal attention network,
visual cortex and occipitotemporal cortex.

The authors interpret this as evidence for neural homophily. People who later
become closer may already be aligned in how they attend to, interpret and
emotionally respond to complex naturalistic stimuli. Some early friendships may
form from circumstance and dissolve, while later-emerging or longer-lasting ties
may reflect deeper interpersonal compatibility.

## Why This Matters

This paper gives friendship neuroscience a temporal handle. Cross-sectional work
can show that friends process the world similarly, but cannot say whether
similarity came before friendship or emerged through shared time. Here, neural
similarity is measured before the social network has formed.

That makes the result useful for thinking about shared reality, prediction and
social attention. Friendship may be partly scaffolded by similar ways of making
sense of the same world. The relevant signal is not just demographics or stated
preferences; it is a dynamic pattern of neural response while people watch
unfolding social, emotional and narrative information.

## Caveats and Limitations

The study is observational, so it cannot prove that neural similarity causes
friendship. Many demographic, biological and sociocultural factors could
contribute to both neural similarity and later social ties. The fMRI sample is
also limited to 41 people from one graduate business cohort at a private US
university, even though the broader social network was fully surveyed.

The design was constrained by the need to scan people quickly after arrival,
which limited sample size. Planned later neuroimaging was blocked by the
COVID-19 pandemic, so the study cannot directly track neural convergence after
friendship. The results may also depend on the movie clips chosen; different
stimuli or resting-state measures may capture different aspects of social
compatibility.

## Key Takeaways

- Incoming students were scanned before they had much chance to form friendships.
- Pre-existing neural similarity predicted social proximity eight months later.
- Future friends were more similar than dyads three degrees apart in left
  orbitofrontal cortex.
- Dyads that grew closer over time showed broader pre-existing neural similarity
  than dyads that drifted apart.
- The paper supports neural homophily while acknowledging that causal mechanisms
  remain unresolved.
