Astrocytic ankyrin-2 enables memory persistence in the mouse hippocampus

July 9, 2026 2026 Nature Communications

Summary

The study asks how astrocytes help memories persist after learning. The authors selectively deleted ankyrin-2 (Ank2) in adult mouse astrocytes and found a specific loss of remote memory while recent memory remained intact. The manipulation also disrupted the maintenance, rather than initial induction, of long-term potentiation.

Mechanistically, Ank2 loss reduced astrocytic contacts with hippocampal engram neurons and impaired astrocyte morphogenesis driven by brain-derived neurotrophic factor. The proposed pathway runs through the astrocytic truncated TrkB receptor, TrkB.T1, and IP3R2-dependent signalling.

Hippocampal BDNF infusion normally strengthened memory persistence, but this effect required astrocytic Ank2. In the complementary sufficiency experiment, the authors developed an optogenetic tool to activate TrkB.T1 signalling in astrocytes and showed that activation enhanced remote memory.

Why This Matters

The work separates memory acquisition from memory persistence and identifies a glial mechanism for the latter. It supports a view in which astrocytes actively remodel their relationship with engram neurons to sustain long-lived synaptic and behavioural change.

Caveats and Limitations

  • The experiments use mice, so relevance to human memory remains to be established.
  • The study focuses on selected hippocampal tasks and may not generalize to every form of memory.
  • Artificial genetic and optogenetic interventions do not reproduce the full dynamics of natural signalling.

Key Takeaways

  • Astrocytic Ank2 is required for remote, but not recent, memory in adult mice.
  • Ank2 supports persistent long-term potentiation and astrocyte-engram contact.
  • BDNF-TrkB.T1-IP3R2 signalling drives the relevant astrocytic structural response.
  • Selective activation of astrocytic TrkB.T1 can enhance remote memory.
