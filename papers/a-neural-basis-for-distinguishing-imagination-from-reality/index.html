Paper Summary

A neural basis for distinguishing imagination from reality

April 17, 2026 2025 Neuron

Summary

This paper asks how the brain decides whether a visual experience came from the outside world or from internal imagination. Participants completed a near-threshold grating-detection task while simultaneously imagining gratings that were either congruent or incongruent with the target signal. Behaviorally, congruent imagery increased “stimulus present” reports, including on trials with no real signal, consistent with imagery pushing experience toward a reality judgment.

The authors formalize this with a reality-threshold model: perceptual and imagined evidence are combined into one latent sensory-strength signal, and a binary reality judgment is made by thresholding that signal. They report that this model explained the data better than alternatives treating imagery and reality judgments as independent.

Neurally, bilateral fusiform gyrus showed the strongest profile expected of this latent reality signal: it tracked imagery vividness and reality judgments, showed sensitivity to perceptual and imagery manipulations, and predicted trial-level imagination-versus-perception confusions. This is conceptually important because it shifts the locus away from early visual cortex toward mid-level visual representations. Frontal regions, especially anterior insula and dmPFC, were more closely tied to the binary decision stage, consistent with a readout/thresholding role.

Taken together, the study moves beyond the broad claim that imagery and perception overlap and offers a more testable neural-computational account: in this controlled visual setting, reality judgments may arise from monitoring and thresholding the combined sensory trace.

Why This Matters

This work provides a concrete mechanism for a central cognitive function: distinguishing internal experience from external events. It also gives a plausible mechanistic bridge to reality-monitoring failures relevant to hallucination research, while appropriately stopping short of claiming that hallucinations are fully explained by this single process.

Caveats and Limitations

  • The final fMRI analysis sample is relatively small (26 participants), so replication is important.
  • The paradigm is intentionally narrow (near-threshold gratings + instructed imagery), which may not capture naturalistic imagination, memory, or hallucinatory experience.
  • Evidence is correlational and model-based, not causal; necessity of fusiform and anterior insula remains to be tested with causal methods.
  • The additive-threshold model is elegant and well-supported in this dataset, but real-world reality monitoring likely also uses context, prior beliefs, agency/source-memory cues, and other control signals.
  • Some effects appear to vary across individuals, suggesting meaningful heterogeneity in susceptibility to imagery-perception confusion.

Key Takeaways

  • A major innovation is a testable reality-threshold mechanism linking behavior, computation, and neural data.
  • Fusiform gyrus (mid-level visual cortex), more than early visual cortex, appears central to the relevant sensory-strength signal in this task.
  • Anterior insula/dmPFC likely support the transition from graded sensory evidence to binary “real vs imagined” reports.
  • The findings are an important mechanistic advance but should not be overgeneralized into a complete theory of reality monitoring.
  • Best interpretation: strong foundational evidence in a tightly controlled visual paradigm, with clear next steps for causal and translational follow-up.
