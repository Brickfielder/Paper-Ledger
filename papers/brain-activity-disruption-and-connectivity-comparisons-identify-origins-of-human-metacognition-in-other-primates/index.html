Paper Summary

Brain activity, disruption and connectivity comparisons identify origins of human metacognition in other primates

May 19, 2026 2026 Nature Human Behaviour

Summary

This paper asks where human prospective metacognition may have come from. Humans can decide whether a future task is worth attempting by estimating both external contingencies and their own likely performance. The authors test whether macaques show a precursor of this ability and which prefrontal circuits support it.

The behavioural task separates two kinds of future probability. Some options depend on the environment, such as the reward probability attached to a stimulus after a correct response. Other options depend on the animal’s own future performance, such as whether it is likely to solve a perceptual decision that will be presented later. Macaques learned to choose between these options before actually performing the later perceptual decision.

The animals could evaluate future environmental probabilities and also showed evidence of estimating their own future performance, especially when the later task was relatively easy. This prospective self-performance estimate was weaker than in humans, but it was not absent.

The neural results identify two complementary macaque prefrontal areas. Activity in area 45a was linked to performance-dependent probability, while activity in area 47/12o was linked to environment-dependent probability. The timing and strength of these signals helped predict which option would be chosen. Causal disruption with transcranial ultrasound produced a double dissociation: area 45a disruption reduced the influence of performance-dependent probability, while area 47/12o disruption reduced the influence of environment-dependent probability. These disruptions affected the prospective choice of which task to attempt, not the later perceptual decision itself.

The final step compares macaque and human prefrontal connectivity. Human anterior lateral prefrontal cortex is associated with more integrated prospective metacognitive evaluation. The connectivity pattern of the combined macaque 45a and 47/12o circuits resembled the human anterior lateral prefrontal profile more than either macaque region alone. The authors therefore propose that human metacognitive planning may elaborate a pair of older primate mechanisms that are more segregated in macaques.

Why This Matters

The paper is valuable because it links three levels that are often studied separately: behaviour, causal brain disruption and comparative connectivity. It does not simply ask whether macaques “have metacognition”; it breaks prospective metacognition into components and maps those components onto prefrontal circuits.

That decomposition is useful. It suggests that human self-evaluation about future actions may be an evolutionary recombination or integration of more basic systems: one estimating what the world will make possible, and another estimating what the agent will be able to do.

Caveats and Limitations

  • The macaque effect is clearest when the future task is easier, so the ability appears limited compared with humans.
  • The task is highly structured and reward-based, which may not capture broader forms of self-reflection or planning.
  • Functional connectivity similarity is not the same as strict anatomical homology.
  • TUS provides causal perturbation evidence, but it does not reveal every synaptic or computational mechanism.
  • Human comparison relies partly on previously collected related task data and connectivity datasets.

Key Takeaways

  • Macaques can prospectively estimate aspects of their own future performance, though less robustly than humans.
  • Area 45a supports evaluation of performance-dependent probability.
  • Area 47/12o supports evaluation of environment-dependent probability.
  • Disrupting each area selectively changes the corresponding prospective judgement without impairing the later perceptual decision itself.
  • Human anterior lateral prefrontal cortex may integrate functions that remain more separated across macaque ventrolateral prefrontal circuits.
