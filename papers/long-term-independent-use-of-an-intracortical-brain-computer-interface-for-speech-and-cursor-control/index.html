Paper Summary

Long-term independent use of an intracortical brain-computer interface for speech and cursor control

June 17, 2026 2026 Nature Medicine

Summary

This Nature Medicine paper reports the long-term independent use of a multimodal intracortical brain-computer interface by a 45-year-old man with paralysis and severe dysarthria due to ALS. Four 64-channel microelectrode arrays were placed in dominant ventral precentral gyrus, including speech motor cortex. Neural activity was decoded in real time into intended words, cursor movements and clicks.

The practical change was the move from researcher-supervised sessions to home use. After care partners were trained to connect and initialize the system, the participant could use the BCI without researchers present. Daily setup still required care-partner help, but once running the system supported independent communication and computer access for long sessions.

Across 19 months, the participant used the system on 444 of 653 days and accumulated more than 3,800 hours of use. He communicated 183,060 sentences, totaling 1,960,163 words, at an average rate of 56 words per minute. He rated 92% of sentences as decoded at least mostly correctly. In formal copy-task benchmarks, attempted speech was decoded with more than 99% word accuracy using a vocabulary of 125,000 words.

The system also supported cursor and click control. A gaze-controlled toggle let the participant switch between interface modes, and decoded speech could be used as keyboard input while cursor decoding provided mouse input. This enabled everyday activities such as messaging, email, browsing, video calls and work.

Why This Matters

Many BCI studies show strong performance in structured research sessions. This paper asks a harder translational question: can a high-performance intracortical BCI keep working in daily life, at home, across many months?

The answer here is a careful yes for one participant and one research system. The long duration, high sentence volume, real-world computer use and low researcher presence make the paper a meaningful step toward practical assistive neurotechnology.

Caveats and Limitations

The evidence is still a single-participant feasibility demonstration. The participant was highly engaged, the system was a research platform, and the hardware involved percutaneous wired connections rather than a fully implanted consumer-ready device.

Care partners remained necessary for setup, and the system benefited from custom software, decoder updates and study-team infrastructure. Generalization to other users, diagnoses, implant locations and long-term clinical deployment will need larger studies.

Key Takeaways

  • A multimodal intracortical BCI supported speech, cursor and click decoding.
  • The participant used the system at home for more than 3,800 hours over 19 months.
  • Personal-use speech decoding produced nearly two million words across 183,060 sentences.
  • Formal speech-copy benchmarks exceeded 99% word accuracy with a large vocabulary.
  • The study demonstrates durable independent use, but not yet broad clinical readiness.
