Paper Summary

Limitations of serial cloning in mammals

April 4, 2026 2026 Nature Communications

Summary

This study followed serial cloning in mice for 20 years starting from a single donor animal. The authors repeatedly used somatic cell nuclear transfer to generate each new generation of cloned mice. Over time, they produced more than 1,200 cloned mice across 58 generations. Early generations showed improving cloning success, but the birth rate later declined and the 58th generation was the last. The cloned mice generally looked normal and had normal lifespans, even at later generations. However, the authors found that large structural and lethal DNA mutations accumulated with each round of cloning. They also observed that embryos from later-generation clones often degenerated after fertilization, even though the oocytes could still be fertilized. In contrast, a few embryos could be normalized by meiosis and fertilization and then develop to term. The study included whole-genome sequencing, histology, immunostaining for epigenetic marks, RNA-seq of blastocysts, and reproductive tests in later-generation mice. The epigenetic state of one-cell embryos and the gene-expression profiles of blastocysts did not show clear accumulation of abnormalities between early and late cloned generations. The authors also found that trichostatin A still improved cloning efficiency, suggesting the later decline was not simply due to loss of drug responsiveness. Overall, the work suggests that mammals may not be able to maintain lineages by serial cloning alone because sexual reproduction helps remove genetic anomalies that clonally reproduced lines accumulate.

Why This Matters

This paper helps explain why cloning in mammals has not become a natural way to maintain a species. It shows that even when cloned animals look healthy, hidden genetic damage can build up across generations and eventually limit reproduction.

Caveats and Limitations

This is a single-lineage mouse study, so the results may not generalize to other mammals or cloning methods. The paper shows a strong association between serial cloning and accumulated DNA damage, but it does not fully separate all possible causes of late-generation failure, including subtle gamete or embryo effects beyond the assays used. The findings are also based on an artificial laboratory setting, which differs from natural reproduction and evolutionary timescales.

Key Takeaways

  • Serial cloning in mice was continued for 58 generations from one donor animal.
  • Cloned mice stayed broadly normal in appearance and lifespan, but genetic defects accumulated over time.
  • Cloning success declined in later generations, and the final generation could no longer be sustained.
  • Late-generation embryos and oocytes still showed some reproductive competence, but many embryos failed after fertilization.
  • Sexual reproduction appeared to restore normal development by clearing some cloning-associated abnormalities.
