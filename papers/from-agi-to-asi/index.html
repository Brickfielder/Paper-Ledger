Paper Summary

From AGI to ASI

June 15, 2026 2026 arXiv

Summary

This report examines what technological development might look like after the arrival of human-level artificial general intelligence. It is not a forecast that AGI or superintelligence will arrive on a particular date. Instead, it organizes plausible pathways, limiting factors, and research questions.

The authors use AGI as shorthand for a system with roughly median-human general cognitive competence, while recognizing that such a system could already be superhuman in selected areas. Their threshold for artificial general superintelligence, or ASI, is much higher: broad capability beyond not only individual humans but large organizations of experts. ASI is therefore not defined by exceptional performance on a narrow set of tasks.

The report describes four pathways, which could operate together:

  1. Scaling compute, models, and data. More effective compute could improve individual systems or make enormous populations of fast AGI instances affordable. The central uncertainty is whether quantitative scaling continues to produce broad qualitative capability gains.
  2. Algorithmic paradigm shifts. New architectures, learning procedures, memory, world models, continual learning, or hardware could break through limits of current foundation-model approaches. Genuine paradigm changes are also the hardest pathway to forecast.
  3. Recursive improvement. AI may accelerate AI research through software, hardware design, data generation, experimentation, and increasingly specialized divisions of labour. Positive feedback could accelerate progress, but physical experiments, growing research difficulty, and other delays may prevent an explosive loop.
  4. Group-agent formation. Coordinated or self-organizing collectives of individually human-level systems might achieve superhuman organizational intelligence. Possible structures include hierarchies, automated firms, and decentralized agent economies.

The authors also describe frictions that affect these pathways differently. These include exhaustion of high-quality human data, energy and hardware demands, increasing difficulty of research, real-world experimental latency, coordination and communication costs, and an “abstraction barrier”: the possibility that systems trained mainly on human-generated data remain bounded by human concepts and struggle to discover genuinely new abstractions from the physical world. Political, legal, commercial, and societal resistance could also constrain technically feasible scaling.

The report uses Universal AI as a theoretical upper reference point. This discussion is meant to emphasize that even extremely capable systems would not be omniscient or omnipotent. Computability, complexity, information, physics, resources, and interaction with the real world all impose limits.

Why This Matters

Discussions of superintelligence often slide between larger models, faster research, recursive self-improvement, and multi-agent collectives as though they were one process. This report separates them and asks what evidence would matter for each.

Its main value is therefore agenda-setting. Questions about data limits, compute-to-capability scaling, automated research, collective intelligence, new concept formation, and physical bottlenecks can be investigated before anyone can confidently predict a post-AGI trajectory.

Caveats and Limitations

The paper is a conceptual map produced by researchers at Google DeepMind. It does not provide an empirical probability for AGI, ASI, or any transition speed. Several quantities used to motivate the discussion, including effective compute growth and algorithmic-efficiency trends, are uncertain and may change quickly.

The definitions of AGI and ASI are deliberately informal and anchored to human performance. Human organizations are themselves variable, and future human-AI institutions could make the comparison unstable. The four pathways overlap, may occur in parallel, and may not cover every important route.

Although the report recognizes wider societal consequences and constraints, its main focus is technological. Governance, environmental costs, inequality, geopolitics, public legitimacy, and the distribution of benefits and harms require much deeper treatment elsewhere.

Key Takeaways

  • AGI and ASI are separated by a deliberately high capability threshold.
  • Scaling, paradigm change, recursive improvement, and agent collectives are distinct pathways.
  • These pathways may overlap and progress at different rates.
  • Data, resources, physical experimentation, abstraction, and coordination may create bottlenecks.
  • The report is best read as a research agenda, not a timeline or prediction.
