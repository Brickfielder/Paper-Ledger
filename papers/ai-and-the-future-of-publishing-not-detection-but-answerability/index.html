AI and the Future of Publishing: Not Detection, but Answerability

July 9, 2026 2026 Philosophy & Technology

Summary

Floridi argues that publishing has concentrated too heavily on provenance tools such as AI-text detectors, declarations, watermarks and attestations. These may say something about how text was produced, but they cannot establish whether its claims are reliable or whether anyone is accountable for them. Detection can also impose a stylistic penalty on non-native English writers.

The proposed alternative is answerability: the standing and obligation to answer for published claims. AI systems cannot hold that standing, which is why they should not be authors. Human participants retain differentiated duties across the publication process, including authors, reviewers, editors and publishers.

Editorial language models fit within this structure as recommenders rather than decision-makers. Their use should involve calibrated reliance, auditing, appeals and human override, with deployment justified by evidence appropriate to the field. Editors therefore remain answerable for when and how these tools shape decisions.

Why This Matters

The paper replaces a brittle policing question, “Was AI used?”, with the more important governance question, “Who can answer for this claim and decision?” That framing can accommodate legitimate AI assistance without transferring scholarly responsibility to a system that cannot bear it.

Caveats and Limitations

  • The article develops a normative framework rather than testing a publishing intervention.
  • Answerability will need concrete procedures that differ across disciplines and editorial roles.
  • Auditing and appeals require resources and may be difficult for smaller publishers to provide.

Key Takeaways

  • AI-text provenance is not a proxy for truth, quality or responsibility.
  • Human authors must remain answerable for every published claim.
  • Editorial AI should recommend, not hold final decision authority.
  • AI-assisted editorial decisions need auditing, appeal and override mechanisms.
