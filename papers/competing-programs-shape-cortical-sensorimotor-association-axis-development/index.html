Paper Summary

Competing programs shape cortical sensorimotor-association axis development

July 2, 2026 2026 Nature

Summary

This study addresses how the cortex develops its sensorimotor-to-association axis: the large-scale gradient from primary sensory and motor areas toward distributed association networks involved in abstract cognition.

The authors propose the multinodal induction-exclusion in network development model, or MIND. In this model, two opposing developmental programs compete for cortical territory. Central programs are induced near future primary areas by first-order thalamocortical input and help establish sensorimotor identities. Pericentral programs emerge around frontal and temporal poles, spread inward and support association-cortex features.

Across transcriptomic, anatomical and experimental evidence, the paper argues that this competition organizes gene expression, axon guidance, cell adhesion, retinoic acid signaling, WNT signaling, synaptogenesis and risk-gene distributions. The PLXNC1-SEMA7A receptor-ligand pair is highlighted as one mechanism by which primary and association corticocortical axons repel one another.

Why This Matters

The sensorimotor-association axis is a core organizing principle of brain maps, development and comparative neuroanatomy. This paper gives that axis a concrete developmental mechanism: not just a gradient that appears in adult data, but a competition between molecular programs and thalamic input during cortical formation.

Caveats and Limitations

  • The framework synthesizes evidence across species, so not every developmental claim is directly observed in the same organism or stage.
  • Transcriptomic programs explain a great deal, but activity, experience, vascular factors and later plasticity may add additional constraints.
  • The clinical links, including autism-risk enrichment, are mechanistic leads rather than direct disease explanations.

Key Takeaways

  • Primary sensorimotor and association cortices may be shaped by opposing developmental programs.
  • Thalamocortical induction helps primary areas form and exclude association identity.
  • Frontotemporal pericentral programs may help build distributed association networks.
  • The model explains why primary areas sit as focal islands within wider association cortex.
