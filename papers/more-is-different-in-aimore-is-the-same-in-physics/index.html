More is Different in AI—More is the Same in Physics

April 6, 2026 2026 Physica A: Statistical Mechanics and its Applications

Summary

The paper revisits Philip W. Anderson’s idea that “more is different” in complex systems and asks how that viewpoint applies to modern machine learning. It compares macroscopic disordered physical systems with finite artificial intelligence models through the lens of spontaneous symmetry breaking. The central claim is that both kinds of systems can show spontaneous symmetry breaking, but the informational meaning of that symmetry breaking differs across the two domains. In the physical setting, the author argues that the state of an individual spin can, in some ordered systems, reflect the equilibrium state of the whole system. For disordered systems such as spin glasses, however, the time-dependent state of one spin or even many spins does not carry useful information about the global equilibrium state. This leads to the characterization of physics in these cases as “More is the Same,” meaning local observations may not reveal the macroscopic state. In contrast, the paper argues that finite AI models behave in a more Anderson-like way, where information about the global task is already present in a single-node symmetry-breaking event. The author further suggests that this information content grows as the number of nodes increases because of cooperation among nodes. On this view, AI exhibits “More is Different” because the collective behavior of the model is not reducible to a simple replication of nodal behavior. The paper frames learning as a process that lies beyond the physical framework described for the spin systems, while still relying on spontaneous symmetry breaking as an organizing mechanism. It argues that learning distributes the task across nodes and thereby avoids simple nodal replication. That distributed structure is presented as a reason complex learning can emerge in finite AI models. Because the article is abstract-only here, the discussion is primarily conceptual rather than based on a detailed empirical test or new benchmark results. The most important methodological point is therefore the analogy between symmetry breaking in statistical physics and in finite AI architectures, rather than a specific experimental protocol. Why This Matters

This paper offers a simple conceptual bridge between physics and AI by asking when local parts do or do not reveal the behavior of the whole. That matters because it gives researchers a language for thinking about why some AI systems show emergent, collective behavior while familiar physical systems may hide global structure from local measurements.

Caveats and Limitations

The argument is largely conceptual and, based on the abstract alone, does not appear to include direct empirical validation across concrete AI architectures or physical models. The comparison also depends on broad analogies between domains, so its conclusions may not generalize uniformly across all kinds of AI systems or disordered physical systems. Without the full text, it is also hard to judge how precisely key terms such as symmetry breaking, cooperation, and learning are defined and operationalized.

Key Takeaways

  • The paper re-examines Anderson’s “More is Different” idea in the context of AI.
  • It contrasts information flow in disordered physics with information flow in finite AI models.
  • Spontaneous symmetry breaking is treated as the shared mechanism linking the two domains.
  • In physics, local states may fail to reveal the global equilibrium state (“More is the Same”).
  • In AI, even local symmetry breaking can contain information about the global task, supporting emergent collective behavior.
