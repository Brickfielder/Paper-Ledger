Paper Summary

Local molecular and global connectomic contributions to cross-disorder cortical abnormalities

July 11, 2026 2022 Nature Communications

Summary

Hansen and colleagues assembled cortical-thickness abnormality maps for thirteen conditions, including ADHD, autism, epilepsy, depression, OCD, schizophrenia, bipolar disorder and Parkinson’s disease. They compared these maps with local molecular annotations, such as gene expression, neurotransmitter receptor density, metabolism and myelination, and with global structural-connectivity properties such as strength, centrality, distance and participation.

Across conditions, molecular features usually explained cortical abnormalities better than global connectivity features. Neurotransmitter receptor profiles were especially strong predictors of both disorder-specific morphology and the degree to which regions were similarly affected across conditions. Regions with similar molecular make-up, particularly receptor and gene-expression profiles, also tended to show similar cross-disorder abnormalities.

The authors do not treat local and global factors as alternatives. Disorders whose cortical patterns were more molecularly predictable also showed stronger network-spreading signatures: a region’s abnormality tracked the abnormality of its structurally and functionally connected neighbours. The resulting cross-disorder epicentre map highlighted bilateral sensory-motor cortex, inferior temporal cortex, precuneus and superior parietal cortex.

Why This Matters

The paper gives a useful multiscale account of transdiagnostic brain abnormalities. It argues that local biology determines which cortical regions are vulnerable, while network wiring helps determine how that vulnerability is distributed across the brain.

Caveats and Limitations

  • The study compares group-average maps and cannot infer patient-specific trajectories.
  • Cross-sectional morphology does not prove trans-synaptic spread or disease causality.
  • Molecular annotations and structural connectomes come from reference datasets rather than the clinical cohorts.
  • The 68-region Desikan-Killiany atlas may miss finer-grained cortical organization.

Key Takeaways

  • Local molecular attributes generally outperform global connectomic metrics as predictors of cortical abnormalities.
  • Receptor profiles and gene-expression similarity track cross-disorder cortical similarity.
  • Local molecular vulnerability and network exposure interact rather than compete.
  • A small set of sensory-motor and association regions recur as cross-disorder network epicentres.
