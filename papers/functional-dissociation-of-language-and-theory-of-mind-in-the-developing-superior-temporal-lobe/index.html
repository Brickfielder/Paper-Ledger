Paper Summary

Functional dissociation of language and theory of mind in the developing superior temporal lobe

April 28, 2026 2026 Communications Biology

Summary

This paper asks whether language and theory of mind (ToM) start from shared neural machinery in childhood and then separate later, or whether they are distinct from early development. Using a child cohort (ages 3-9; 54 sessions from 42 children) and an adult comparison group (n = 28), the authors report that core superior temporal language and ToM regions are already functionally dissociated in children in a pattern similar to adults.

They also test developmental “disentangling” directly. Across both cross-sectional analyses and longitudinal follow-up, they find little evidence that non-preferred responses (e.g., ToM responses in language regions) systematically decrease with age in core regions over this window. In parallel, connectivity-fingerprint analyses indicate that connections predicting language versus ToM activation are largely non-overlapping and temporally stable.

Overall, the study argues that language and ToM do not primarily arise by progressive separation from one common social processor in superior temporal cortex during early childhood. Instead, distinct domain organization appears early, with ongoing specialization layered on top.

Strengths

  • Strong developmental framing with both child and adult data, plus longitudinal analyses in children.
  • Within-study comparison of language and ToM in the same broad anatomical territory (superior temporal lobe) improves interpretability of dissociation claims.
  • Converging evidence from both functional selectivity and connectivity-based prediction strengthens the core inference.
  • Explicitly tests (rather than assumes) the popular developmental-disentangling hypothesis.

Caveats and Limitations

  • The age range is focused on early childhood (3-9 years), so later developmental reorganization in adolescence is not addressed.
  • fMRI selectivity and functional connectivity fingerprints are correlational; they cannot by themselves identify circuit-level causal mechanisms.
  • Some marginal regions show mixed responses, so dissociation appears strongest in core parcels rather than uniformly across all superior temporal subregions.
  • Behavioral coupling between language and ToM development can still occur even if core neural substrates are already distinct.

Take-Home Message

Language and theory-of-mind representations in superior temporal cortex look more “early-distinct” than “late-separated”: by early childhood, their core neural signatures are already dissociable, and connectivity fingerprints for each domain are largely independent.
