Paper Summary

Perineuronal nets in cerebellar nuclei neurons orchestrate social behaviour via regulation of neuronal activity in circuits innervated by the cerebellum

June 15, 2026 2026 Translational Psychiatry

Summary

Perineuronal nets, or PNNs, are extracellular-matrix structures surrounding some neurons. They help regulate excitability, synaptic signalling, and plasticity. This study asks whether PNNs around neurons in the deep cerebellar nuclei contribute to social behaviour and whether their disruption resembles features seen in mouse models associated with autism.

The authors first measured PNN labelling in two models: mice exposed prenatally to valproic acid and mice with one disrupted copy of the autism-associated gene Chd8. Both showed reduced PNN labelling in cerebellar-nuclei neurons.

To test whether this change could influence behaviour, the researchers injected chondroitinase ABC into the deep cerebellar nuclei to degrade PNN components. Treated mice showed less interaction in social tests and changes in some anxiety-related measures. The treatment did not simply eliminate movement, but the behavioural findings are not entirely specific to sociability.

Several experiments linked the behavioural effect to altered neural activity. Large glutamatergic neurons in the cerebellar nuclei normally increased calcium activity during social interaction. This response, along with activity-related CREB1 phosphorylation, was reduced after PNN disruption. Activity markers also fell in connected regions including the red nucleus and ventromedial thalamus.

The transcription factor ARNT2 was elevated in cerebellar-nuclei neurons after PNN disruption and in both autism-associated mouse models. This increase was present at baseline rather than being a normal activity-dependent response. Reducing ARNT2 with an AAV-delivered shRNA partly restored social interaction, social preference, CREB1 phosphorylation, and c-Fos activity in several downstream regions, despite not restoring the PNNs themselves.

Why This Matters

The paper strengthens the case that the cerebellum contributes to distributed social and affective processing rather than serving only motor coordination. It also proposes a mechanistic sequence linking extracellular matrix, cerebellar output-neuron activity, downstream circuits, and behaviour.

The ARNT2 rescue experiment is particularly useful because it moves beyond an association between reduced PNNs and behaviour. It suggests that altered activity regulation downstream of PNN disruption may be one part of the causal pathway.

Caveats and Limitations

This is a preclinical mouse study. Valproate exposure and Chd8 haploinsufficiency reproduce selected biological and behavioural features, but neither is equivalent to autism in people. Terms such as social impairment refer to specific mouse assays and should not be read as direct models of human social experience.

Only male mice were used. Several imaging and histological analyses involved small groups, sometimes three mice per condition, and the methods state that sample selection was not randomized for behavioural or histological experiments. These features increase the risk of unstable estimates or bias.

Chondroitinase ABC broadly degrades PNN components and may trigger effects not captured by the proposed PNN-ARNT2 pathway. The study also does not establish why valproate exposure or Chd8 disruption reduced PNNs. Replication across models, sexes, laboratories, and more selective manipulations is needed, as is direct evidence concerning PNNs and ARNT2 in human autism.

Key Takeaways

  • Cerebellar-nuclei PNN labelling was reduced in two autism-associated mouse models.
  • Local PNN disruption altered social behaviour and distributed neural activity.
  • Social interaction normally activated large glutamatergic cerebellar-nuclei neurons.
  • ARNT2 increased after PNN disruption and in both mouse models.
  • ARNT2 knockdown partly rescued behaviour and activity markers without restoring PNNs.
