Paper Summary

Lifespan development of EEG alpha and aperiodic component sources is shaped by the connectome and axonal delays

April 25, 2026 2026 National Science Review

Summary

This paper asks how two major components of resting EEG, aperiodic activity and alpha rhythms, develop across the human lifespan. The authors introduce xi-alphaNET, a model that treats these components as stochastic cortical processes constrained by anatomical connectivity and axonal conduction delays.

Using cross-spectral resting-state EEG from 1,965 participants aged 5 to 100 in the HarMNqEEG dataset, the model estimates source activity, effective connectivity, and conduction delays with high test-retest reliability. The inferred organization separates a frontal aperiodic component from a posterior alpha component. These components also show opposite directional patterns when interpreted through a cortical hierarchy derived from T1w/T2w myelination maps.

The main developmental result is nonlinear change. Spectral parameters follow an inverted-U trajectory across life, rather than changing monotonically from childhood to ageing. The paper also reports that modelling delays and connectome constraints helps explain how large-scale anatomy shapes the observable EEG spectrum.

Why This Matters

EEG is easy to collect but hard to interpret mechanistically. This study is valuable because it connects familiar spectral quantities to source-level processes, anatomical connectivity, directionality, and development. It gives a way to ask how maturation and ageing alter the generators of EEG rhythms, not just their scalp-level appearance.

Caveats and Limitations

The results depend on the modelling framework. The source estimates, feedforward and feedback interpretations, and delay parameters are inferred through assumptions about connectivity, myelination, and stochastic process structure. The model is powerful, but it should be read as a mechanistic interpretation of EEG rather than a direct measurement of neuronal populations.

Key Takeaways

  • xi-alphaNET separates EEG aperiodic and alpha components under connectome and delay constraints.
  • The study analyzes a large lifespan EEG sample spanning ages 5 to 100.
  • Aperiodic sources are emphasized in frontal cortex, while alpha sources are emphasized posteriorly.
  • Both components follow nonlinear lifespan trajectories.
  • The paper strengthens the link between EEG spectral features and anatomical brain organization.
