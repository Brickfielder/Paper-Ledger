Paper Summary

Induction of cortical on/off periods in awake mice fulfills sleep functions

June 15, 2026 2026 Nature Neuroscience

Summary

This study asks whether the alternating neuronal on and off periods associated with NREM sleep actively perform sleep functions, or merely accompany them. The authors used optogenetics to induce sleep-like off periods in the cortex of awake adult mice while recording local field potentials, neuronal firing and muscle activity.

After five hours of sleep deprivation, the researchers induced cortical on/off periods during the final 30 minutes while keeping the animals awake. They used two complementary models: activating somatostatin-positive inhibitory neurons, and directly inhibiting excitatory pyramidal neurons. Both approaches produced local network bistability resembling the alternation between silent off periods and active on periods seen in NREM sleep.

During subsequent recovery sleep, the stimulated cortical region showed lower slow-wave activity and less neuronal synchrony than the corresponding region in the opposite hemisphere. These are signs that some local sleep pressure had already been discharged. The difference faded after the first few hours of recovery sleep. Wild-type control animals showed no comparable effect.

A particularly useful control separated network pattern from reduced activity alone. Tonic optogenetic inhibition lowered neuronal firing by a similar amount but did not create alternating on/off periods and did not reduce subsequent slow-wave activity or synchrony. This suggests that the temporal organization of cortical activity, rather than simply firing less, was responsible for the sleep-like effect.

The authors then examined molecular markers associated with synaptic strength. Broad unilateral induction during the end of sleep deprivation reduced synaptic GluA1-containing AMPA receptors and phosphorylation of GluA1 at serine 845 in the stimulated hemisphere. The direction and scale of these changes resembled those observed after natural sleep, supporting a role for on/off activity in synaptic homeostasis.

Finally, the researchers tested novel floor-texture recognition, a memory task that depends on sensorimotor cortex. Sleep deprivation impaired later recognition, whereas bilateral induction of on/off periods over the relevant cortical regions during deprivation restored performance to the level of mice allowed to sleep.

Why This Matters

Sleep slow waves have long been associated with the restoration of neural networks, but association alone cannot establish whether the underlying on/off dynamics are doing the restorative work. By creating those dynamics locally in awake animals, this study provides causal evidence that they can reduce local sleep need, promote molecular signs of synaptic down-selection and support consolidation of a specific cortical memory.

The findings strengthen the concept of local sleep: sleep pressure is not only a single brain-wide state, but can partly reflect the recent activity and recovery of individual cortical networks. The tonic-inhibition control is important because it indicates that structured alternation between activity and silence matters more than a simple reduction in firing rate.

Caveats and Limitations

  • The work was performed in mice using invasive optogenetic methods, so it does not establish a safe or effective intervention for humans.
  • The experiments used several modest-sized cohorts and sample sizes were not determined through prospective statistical calculations.
  • Optogenetic collection and analysis could not be fully blinded because the stimulation protocol was apparent, although sleep scoring was blinded where possible.
  • The induced patterns were not identical to natural NREM sleep. One model differed in slow-wave polarity, and induced periods did not reproduce the full spindle activity of subsequent sleep.
  • GluA1 and phosphorylated GluA1 are indirect molecular indicators rather than direct structural or electrophysiological measurements of synaptic strength.
  • Memory recovery was shown in one sensorimotor recognition task. The findings do not imply that local stimulation can replace the brain-wide and systemic functions of sleep.

Key Takeaways

  • Sleep-like cortical on/off periods can be induced locally while mice remain awake.
  • Induction reduced local slow-wave activity and neuronal synchrony during subsequent recovery sleep.
  • Merely lowering neuronal firing without network bistability did not produce the same result.
  • Induced on/off periods reproduced molecular changes associated with sleep and synaptic down-selection.
  • Stimulation restored performance in a sleep-dependent sensorimotor memory task, but did not reproduce or replace sleep as a whole.
