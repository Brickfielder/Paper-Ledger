Paper Summary

Frontal cortex organization supporting audiovisual processing during naturalistic viewing

June 25, 2026 2026 Nature Communications

Summary

This Nature Communications paper asks how the human frontal cortex represents auditory and visual information during natural movie viewing. The authors recorded intracranial EEG from 19 neurosurgical participants while they watched a short multilingual film containing English speech, foreign-language speech with subtitles, non-speech sounds and silent visual scenes.

The analysis focused on high-gamma activity, using both unsupervised clustering and supervised encoding models. Neural responses during the movie separated into auditory and visual patterns. In perceptual cortex, auditory responses were prominent in superior temporal regions and visual responses in occipital regions. In frontal cortex, the same modalities appeared later and with a spatial organization.

The key result is a ventral-to-dorsal gradient in lateral frontal cortex. Ventrolateral prefrontal electrodes were more auditory-selective, while dorsolateral prefrontal electrodes were more visual-selective. Encoding models suggested that this gradient was driven mainly by high-level features extracted from transformer-based auditory and visual models, not simply by low-level spectral or motion features.

The organization was also context dependent. During English scenes, frontal encoding leaned more auditory; during foreign-language scenes with subtitles, it leaned more visual. Separate behavioral ratings from online participants captured when audio or visual information was more important for movie understanding, and neural encoding of this audiovisual assignment was strongest in frontal cortex.

Why This Matters

The paper treats multisensory perception as a dynamic resource-allocation problem. In real life, the brain does not just combine audio and vision in a fixed way. It changes which stream matters depending on what the situation requires.

Intracranial EEG makes the timing especially useful. The results suggest that frontal cortex represents high-level audiovisual information later than perceptual regions, while also tracking which modality should be weighted for comprehension.

Caveats and Limitations

The data come from neurosurgical patients, so electrode coverage is determined by clinical needs rather than an even sampling plan. That is normal for iEEG work, but it limits anatomical completeness.

The movie is richer than a standard task but still only one structured naturalistic stimulus. The encoding models also show prediction and alignment between features and neural activity; they do not by themselves prove that the frontal signals causally control audiovisual integration.

Key Takeaways

  • Intracranial EEG was recorded while 19 participants watched a multilingual movie.
  • Auditory and visual representations extended from perceptual cortices into frontal cortex.
  • Lateral frontal cortex showed a ventral-auditory to dorsal-visual gradient.
  • The gradient was driven mainly by high-level audiovisual features.
  • Frontal representations shifted with context, supporting flexible audiovisual-resource assignment.
